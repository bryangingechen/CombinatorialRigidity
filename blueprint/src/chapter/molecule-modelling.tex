\section{Projective duality and the molecule modelling equivalence}
\label{sec:molecule-modelling}

The molecule application of the conjecture rests on a chain of three
equivalent rigidity models for a graph $G$ of minimum degree at least
two, all in $\R^3$:
\[
  \text{bar-joint framework of } G^2
  \;\Longleftrightarrow\;
  \text{molecular (hinge-concurrent body-hinge) framework of } G
  \;\Longleftrightarrow\;
  \text{panel-hinge framework of } G .
\]
The first equivalence is the modelling fact that a molecule --- atoms
joined by covalent bonds --- is a bar-joint framework on the square
graph exactly when it is a body-hinge framework whose hinges at each
atom meet at the atom's centre (Whiteley~\cite{whiteley1999}, in the
form used by Jackson--Jord\'an~\cite{jacksonJordan2008}). The second is
projective duality: in $\R^3$ the projective dual sends points to
planes and lines to lines, carrying a hinge-concurrent body-hinge
framework to a panel-hinge framework, and infinitesimal rigidity is
invariant under it (Crapo--Whiteley~\cite{crapoWhiteley1982}, \S3.6).

This chapter states both links at the level of \emph{motion-space
dimensions} (equivalently, rigidity-matrix ranks), not merely as
realizability equivalences. The stronger form is what the rank formula
needs: composed with Theorem~5.6 (\cref{thm:theorem-55-6}, at $d = 3$
\cref{thm:theorem-55-6-d3}) and the generic $3$-dimensional bar-joint
rigidity matroid of \cref{sec:bar-joint-3d}, the dimension-carrying
chain yields the molecule rank formula
$r(G^2) = 3|V| - 6 - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$
(KT Corollary~5.7, due to Jackson--Jord\'an~\cite{jacksonJordan2008}),
assembled in the final chapter with no further combinatorial input.
(With only the realizability equivalences, the rank formula would
instead require the deficiency induction and independent-cover bounds
of~\cite{jacksonJordan2008}, a substantial separate development.) All
the geometry here is specific to $\R^3$: it is where the projective
dual transposes points and planes and where the molecule model lives
(KT \S1.2 and the reconciliation on p.~671).

\subsection{The square graph and molecular frameworks}
\label{sec:molecule-modelling-defs}

\begin{definition}[Square of a graph]
  \label{def:square-graph}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.square}
  \leanok
  The \emph{square} of a graph $G = (V,E)$ is the graph
  $G^2 = (V, E^2)$ with
  \[
    E^2 = E \cup \{\, uv \mid u \ne v,\ \exists w \in V \setminus \{u,v\}
      \text{ with } uw, wv \in E \,\},
  \]
  i.e.\ $G$ together with an edge for every pair of vertices at
  distance two.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Closed neighbourhoods are cliques of the square]
  \label{lem:square-cliques}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.isClique_closedNeighborSet_square,
        SimpleGraph.exists_mem_closedNeighborSet_of_square_adj}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:square-graph}
  For every vertex $v$ of $G$, the closed neighbourhood
  $N[v] = \{v\} \cup N(v)$ induces a complete subgraph of $G^2$; and
  every edge of $G^2$ lies inside $N[v]$ for some $v$ (an original edge
  $uv$ inside $N[u]$; a distance-two edge $uw$ through the common
  neighbour $v$ inside $N[v]$).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  Two neighbours of $v$ are at distance at most two through $v$, and
  $v$ is adjacent to each of its neighbours; both cases land in $E^2$.
  The covering statement is the case analysis in the parenthesis.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Molecular (hinge-concurrent) framework]
  \label{def:hinge-concurrent}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.molecularOfCentres}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:hinge-constraint}
  Let $G$ be a multigraph with an endpoint selector and
  $c : V \to \R^3$ an assignment of a \emph{centre} to each body. The
  \emph{molecular framework} on $(G, c)$ is the body-hinge framework
  (\cref{def:hinge-constraint}) whose hinge at each edge $e = uv$ is
  the line through the two endpoint centres: its supporting
  $2$-extensor is $\hat c(u) \vee \hat c(v)$, the join of the
  homogenized centres. Every hinge incident to $v$ passes through
  $c(v)$ by construction, which is the \emph{hinge-concurrency} KT
  describe on p.~671; conversely, when the centres are distinct, a
  hinge through both $c(u)$ and $c(v)$ \emph{is} the line
  $c(u)c(v)$, so carrying the centres as data loses no generality.
  A molecule (atoms as bodies, bonds as hinges) is modelled by such a
  framework~\cite{jacksonJordan2008,whiteley1999}: each atom's bonds
  emanate from the atom's centre. When two centres coincide the join
  degenerates to zero (a welded hinge); the genuine-hinge statements
  below therefore carry an injectivity or general-position hypothesis
  on $c$.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Projective invariance and duality}
\label{sec:molecule-modelling-projective}

\begin{theorem}[Projective invariance of infinitesimal rigidity;
  Crapo--Whiteley]
  \label{thm:projective-invariance}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.mapExtensor,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.infinitesimalMotions_mapExtensor,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.finrank_infinitesimalMotions_mapExtensor,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.rankHypothesis_mapExtensor,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.isInfinitesimallyRigid_mapExtensor,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.supportExtensor_mapExtensor_ne_zero,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.infinitesimalMotions_scaleExtensor}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:hinge-constraint, def:rank-hypothesis}
  Let $\Lambda$ be a linear automorphism of the screw space
  $\textstyle\bigwedge^2 \R^4$ and $F$ a body-hinge framework in
  $\R^3$. Transporting $F$ along $\Lambda$ --- keeping the multigraph
  and replacing each supporting extensor $C(p(e))$ by
  $\Lambda\,C(p(e))$ --- carries the infinitesimal-motion space
  $Z(G,p)$ to its image under the bodywise map
  $S \mapsto \Lambda \circ S$. In particular the motion-space
  dimension, the realized rank, and infinitesimal rigidity are
  unchanged. This applies to the linear map on screws induced by any
  projective transformation of $\R^3$ --- collineation or correlation
  (polarity) --- which is Crapo--Whiteley's projective invariance of
  infinitesimal rigidity (\cite{crapoWhiteley1982}, \S3.6); the
  correlation case is the one the duality below consumes. Motions are
  likewise unchanged when each supporting extensor is rescaled by a
  nonzero scalar (the normalization from homogeneous to affine data),
  since only the spans of the extensors enter the constraints.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  A screw assignment $S$ satisfies the hinge constraint
  $S(u) - S(v) \in \langle C(p(e)) \rangle$ if and only if
  $\Lambda S(u) - \Lambda S(v) \in \langle \Lambda C(p(e)) \rangle$,
  because $\Lambda$ is linear and invertible; so
  $S \mapsto \Lambda \circ S$ is a linear isomorphism between the two
  motion spaces. Trivial motions (constant screw assignments) map to
  trivial motions, so rigidity transfers; dimensions transfer along the
  isomorphism. This is Crapo--Whiteley's argument verbatim: a
  projective transformation ``is expressed by a non-singular linear
  transformation on the projective coordinates,'' inducing a linear
  transformation of the screw space that carries instantaneous motions
  to instantaneous motions and has an inverse.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Projective dual of a panel-hinge framework]
  \label{lem:panel-hinge-dual-molecular}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.screwComplementIso,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.screwComplementIso_lineExtensor,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.molecularOfCentres_mapExtensor_screwComplementIso,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.finrank_infinitesimalMotions_ofNormals_homogenize,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.rankHypothesis_ofNormals_homogenize_iff,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.isInfinitesimallyRigid_ofNormals_homogenize_iff,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.supportExtensor_ofNormals_homogenize_ne_zero_iff}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-hinge-framework, def:panel-support-extensor,
        def:meet-complement-iso, def:hinge-concurrent,
        thm:projective-invariance}
  Work in $\R^3$ with the polarity that matches a plane's homogeneous
  normal $4$-vector with the homogeneous coordinates of its pole, so a
  panel $\Pi(v) = \{x \mid x \cdot c(v) + 1 = 0\}$ with normal
  $n_v = (c(v), 1)$ has pole $c(v)$ (KT \S5.1). For a panel-hinge
  framework with normals $n_v$, the supporting extensor of the hinge at
  $e = uv$ is by construction the complement isomorphism applied to the
  join of the two normals (\cref{def:panel-support-extensor}); the join
  $n_u \wedge n_v$ itself is the supporting extensor of the line
  through the two poles, i.e.\ of the molecular framework on the
  centres $c$ (\cref{def:hinge-concurrent}). Hence the panel-hinge
  framework on normals $(c(v), 1)$ and the molecular framework on
  centres $c(v)$ are transports of one another along the fixed screw
  automorphism of \cref{thm:projective-invariance} with
  $\Lambda$ the complement isomorphism (\cref{def:meet-complement-iso}):
  their motion spaces have equal dimension, their realized ranks agree,
  and hinges are genuine on one side iff on the other. For normals with
  nonvanishing last coordinate the same holds after the rescaling
  normalization of \cref{thm:projective-invariance}.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  The panel support extensor is defined as the complement isomorphism
  of the normals' join, so the extensor-level identity is definitional;
  \cref{thm:projective-invariance} with $\Lambda$ the complement
  isomorphism (a fixed linear equivalence of
  $\textstyle\bigwedge^2 \R^4$) transports motions, dimensions, and
  genuineness. Hinge-concurrency of the dual is structural: the join
  $\hat c(u) \vee \hat c(v)$ visibly contains each endpoint's
  homogenized centre as a factor, i.e.\ the dual hinge passes through
  both poles (KT~\cite{katohTanigawa2011}, p.~671).
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Panel-hinge $\Leftrightarrow$ molecular, rank-carrying]
  \label{thm:panel-hinge-iff-molecular}
  \uses{thm:projective-invariance, lem:panel-hinge-dual-molecular}
  For every multigraph $G$ and every target dimension $z$: $G$ has a
  nonparallel panel-hinge realization in $\R^3$ with
  $\dim Z(G,p) = z$ and poles in general position if and only if $G$
  has a molecular realization with $\dim Z(G,p^\ast) = z$ and centres
  in general position. In particular (taking
  $z = D$) $G$ is realizable as an infinitesimally rigid panel-hinge
  framework iff as an infinitesimally rigid molecular framework ---
  the realizability form KT state on p.~671.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  Both directions are \cref{lem:panel-hinge-dual-molecular}: the
  correspondence normals $\leftrightarrow$ centres is a bijection on
  the general-position realizations in both directions (the polarity is
  an involution up to the normalization rescaling), and it preserves
  the motion-space dimension.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Screw velocity fields}
\label{sec:molecule-modelling-velocity}

The square-graph dictionary compares screw assignments (one screw per
body) with joint velocities (one vector per atom centre). The
translation is the classical velocity field of a screw.

\begin{definition}[Velocity field of a screw]
  \label{def:screw-velocity}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.screwVel,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.screwOmega,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.screwTau}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:hinge-constraint}
  Write a screw $S \in \bigwedge^2 \R^4$ in graded Pl\"ucker
  coordinates $(\omega_S, t_S) \in \R^3 \times \R^3$ (the components
  pairing two affine directions, resp.\ a direction with the
  homogenizing coordinate). The \emph{velocity field} of $S$ is
  \[
    \mathrm{vel}_S(x) = \omega_S \times x + t_S \qquad (x \in \R^3),
  \]
  linear in $S$. For a line $2$-extensor
  $\hat a \vee \hat b$ this is the familiar rotation field about the
  line through $a$ and $b$: $\omega = b - a$, $t = a \times b$.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Lines through two points, velocity form]
  \label{lem:screw-velocity-line}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.screwVel_eq_zero_iff_mem_span,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.dotProduct_screwVel_sub}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:screw-velocity, def:hinge-concurrent}
  For distinct $a, b \in \R^3$ and any screw $S$:
  $\mathrm{vel}_S(a) = 0$ and $\mathrm{vel}_S(b) = 0$ if and only if
  $S$ is a scalar multiple of $\hat a \vee \hat b$. Moreover every
  velocity field is infinitesimally isometric:
  $\langle x - y,\ \mathrm{vel}_S(x) - \mathrm{vel}_S(y) \rangle = 0$
  for all $x, y$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  The isometry identity is
  $\langle x - y, \omega \times (x - y) \rangle = 0$. If
  $\mathrm{vel}_S(a) = 0$ then $t = a \times \omega$, and
  $\mathrm{vel}_S(b) = \omega \times (b - a) = 0$ forces $\omega$
  parallel to $b - a$; the solutions form the one-dimensional span of
  $\hat a \vee \hat b$, which conversely vanishes at $a$ and $b$ by the
  formula in \cref{def:screw-velocity}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[A screw is determined by point velocities]
  \label{lem:screw-determination}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.eq_zero_of_screwVel_eq_zero,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.existsUnique_screwVel_eq}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:screw-velocity, lem:screw-velocity-line}
  A screw whose velocity field vanishes at three non-collinear points
  is zero. Consequently, if a finite point family contains three
  non-collinear points, every point of the family is affinely
  independent from each such triple, and a velocity assignment
  satisfies the bar constraint
  $\langle p_i - p_j,\ x_i - x_j \rangle = 0$ for \emph{every} pair,
  then there is a unique screw $S$ with $\mathrm{vel}_S(p_i) = x_i$
  for all $i$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  Vanishing at two points puts $S$ on the line span
  (\cref{lem:screw-velocity-line}); vanishing at a third non-collinear
  point kills the span, which gives uniqueness. For existence, first
  solve on a non-collinear triple $q_0, q_1, q_2$. Write the target as
  the edge differences $d_i = x_i - x_0$ against $e_i = q_i - q_0$; the
  three bar constraints read $e_1 \cdot d_1 = e_2 \cdot d_2 = 0$ and
  $e_1 \cdot d_2 + e_2 \cdot d_1 = 0$. Then
  $\omega_0 = (e_1 \cdot e_1)^{-1}\,(e_1 \times d_1)$ solves
  $\omega \times e_1 = d_1$ by the vector triple product, and adding
  the unique multiple of $e_1$ that also fixes the second edge — a
  multiple of $e_1 \times e_2$, precisely because of the third bar
  constraint — yields an $\omega$ with $\omega \times e_i = d_i$; take
  $S$ with rotation part $\omega$ and the matching translation part.
  For any further point $w$, the residual velocity after subtracting
  the triangle's screw is orthogonal to $w - q_0, w - q_1, w - q_2$,
  which span $\R^3$ because $\{w, q_0, q_1, q_2\}$ is affinely
  independent; so the residual is zero. The affine-independence
  hypothesis is sharp: four coplanar points admit the out-of-plane flex
  of a flat complete quadrilateral, which is not a screw restriction.
\end{proof}

\subsection{General position}
\label{sec:molecule-modelling-general-position}

\begin{definition}[General position up to order four]
  \label{def:general-position-placement}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.IsGeneralPositionPlacement}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:framework}
  A placement $p : V \to \R^3$ is \emph{in general position up to
  order four} if every subfamily of at most four points is affinely
  independent (so in particular $p$ is injective and no three points
  are collinear). Points on the moment curve
  $t \mapsto (t, t^2, t^3)$ at distinct parameters witness the
  condition, by Vandermonde determinants.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[Generic placements in general position]
  \label{lem:exists-generic-general-position}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.exists_isGenericPlacement_isGeneralPositionPlacement}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:general-position-placement, def:generic-placement}
  For every finite $V$ there is a placement $p : V \to \R^3$ that is
  simultaneously generic for row independence
  (\cref{def:generic-placement}) and in general position up to order
  four.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  Re-run the finite-family interpolation of
  \cref{lem:exists-generic-placement} with the finitely many
  order-$\le 4$ affine-independence conditions added to the avoided
  sets: along an affine path of placements each condition is the
  nonvanishing of a polynomial in the path parameter, not identically
  zero by the moment-curve witness, hence with finitely many roots.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Theorem~5.6 at $d = 3$, general-position form]
  \label{lem:theorem-56-general-position}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.exists_rankHypothesis_isGeneralPosition4}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:general-position-placement}
  Let $G$ be a simple spanning graph on at least one body. Then $G$
  has a panel-hinge realization in $\R^3$ with
  $\dim Z(G,p) = D + \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ whose normals have
  nonvanishing last coordinate and whose poles are in general position
  up to order four.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55-6-d3, lem:genericity-device,
        lem:trivial-motions-rank-bound}
  Dispatch on the number of bodies. On a single body $G$ is edgeless
  (a loopless graph on one vertex), so $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$,
  the realization is trivially rigid, and a moment-curve normal is
  vacuously in general position up to order four.

  With at least two bodies, start from the genuine realization of
  \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-d3}. Its realized rank
  $\dim Z = D + \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ is, through the
  rigidity-row/motion complement identity, an exact count of
  $D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ independent rigidity rows;
  since the row coordinates are polynomials in the normal coordinates,
  the independence is witnessed by a single nonzero rational minor
  polynomial (the mechanism of \cref{lem:genericity-device}). Multiply it
  by the order-four general-position avoidance polynomial --- the product
  of the last-coordinate variables $\prod_a X_{a,3}$ with, over the
  injective vertex tuples of sizes $2, 3, 4$, the leading square minors of
  the normal matrix, each nonzero at the moment-curve normals by the
  Vandermonde determinant. Both factors are nonzero rational polynomials,
  so an assignment whose coordinates are algebraically independent over
  $\Q$ is a simultaneous non-root. There the realized rank is at least the
  witnessed value, the reverse inequality is the deterministic bound
  $D + \operatorname{def}(\tilde G) \le \dim Z$ for genuine-hinge
  frameworks (\cref{lem:trivial-motions-rank-bound}, whose genuine hinges
  come from the general position), and the normals satisfy every
  general-position condition. KT compress this strengthening into the word
  ``nonparallel'' (p.~671); the order-four form is what the dictionary
  below consumes.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The square-graph dictionary}
\label{sec:molecule-modelling-equivalences}

\begin{theorem}[Molecule $\Leftrightarrow$ bar-joint of the square;
  Whiteley, Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{thm:molecular-iff-square-bar-joint}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.molecular_finrank_motions_eq_square_ker}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:hinge-concurrent, def:square-graph, lem:square-cliques,
        def:framework, lem:screw-velocity-line,
        lem:screw-determination, def:general-position-placement}
  Let $G = (V,E)$ be a graph of minimum degree at least two and
  $c : V \to \R^3$ a placement in general position up to order four.
  Then the infinitesimal-motion space of the molecular framework on
  $(G, c)$ (\cref{def:hinge-concurrent}) and the kernel of the
  bar-joint rigidity matrix of $(G^2, c)$ are isomorphic as vector
  spaces:
  \[
    \dim Z_{\mathrm{mol}}(G, c) \;=\; \dim \ker R(G^2, c),
    \qquad\text{hence}\qquad
    \operatorname{rank} R(G^2, c) = 3|V| - \dim Z_{\mathrm{mol}}(G, c).
  \]
  In particular $G$ has an infinitesimally rigid molecular realization
  iff $G^2$ has an infinitesimally rigid bar-joint realization
  (Whiteley's modelling equivalence, for which
  Jackson--Jord\'an~\cite{jacksonJordan2008} is the citable published
  anchor; KT resolve the conjecture that closes the argument).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  The isomorphism sends a screw assignment $S$ to the velocity
  assignment $v \mapsto \mathrm{vel}_{S(v)}(c(v))$. It lands in
  $\ker R(G^2, c)$: an original edge splits as one screw's isometry
  plus a hinge term vanishing at the endpoint
  (\cref{lem:screw-velocity-line}); a distance-two edge $uw$ through
  $v$ is handled entirely by body $v$'s screw, since the hinges
  $c(u)c(v)$ and $c(w)c(v)$ let $S(v)$ reproduce the velocities at
  both neighbours. It is injective: a motion in the kernel has each
  screw's velocity field vanishing on the $\ge 3$ non-collinear points
  of its closed neighbourhood (minimum degree two plus general
  position), so each screw is zero (\cref{lem:screw-determination}).
  It is surjective: a bar-joint motion of $G^2$ restricts to a
  pairwise-constrained velocity assignment on each closed-neighbourhood
  clique (\cref{lem:square-cliques}), which determines a unique screw
  per body (\cref{lem:screw-determination}); adjacent bodies' screws
  differ by a screw vanishing at both endpoint centres, i.e.\ by a
  multiple of the hinge line (\cref{lem:screw-velocity-line}), so the
  family is a molecular motion mapping back to the given one. The rank
  identity is rank--nullity; the realizability corollary is the
  dimension statement at
  $\dim Z_{\mathrm{mol}} = D = 6$ together with the existence of
  general-position placements on each side.
\end{proof}
