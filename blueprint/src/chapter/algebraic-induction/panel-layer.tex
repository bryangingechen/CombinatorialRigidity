\subsection{Panel-hinge frameworks}
\label{sec:molecular-algebraic-induction-panel}

Katoh--Tanigawa's \emph{panel-hinge} framework is a hinge-coplanar
body-hinge framework: at each body all incident hinges lie in a common
hyperplane \cite[p.\,647]{katohTanigawa2011}. The molecular conjecture
asserts that this coplanarity can be imposed without losing rigidity. The
body-hinge framework of \cref{def:hinge-constraint} carries free hinges
with no coplanarity constraint, so the constructions below fix the
coplanarity through explicit panel data. A panel realization assigns each
body $v$ a hyperplane normal $n_v \in \R^{k+2}$, and the hinge at an edge
$e = uv$ is the codimension-$2$ intersection $\mathrm{panel}(u) \cap
\mathrm{panel}(v)$. Its supporting $k$-extensor --- the screw-space element
of \cref{def:hinge-constraint} that the rigidity matrix constrains --- is
the Grassmann--Cayley meet of the two panels, equivalently the complement
iso of the join of the two normals. Both coplanarity and transversality are
then expressed in the extensor algebra, so no affine-subspace-intersection
machinery is needed.

\begin{definition}[Panel support extensor]
  \label{def:panel-support-extensor}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.panelSupportExtensor}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:join, def:meet-complement-iso, def:hinge-constraint}
  For two panel normals $n_1, n_2 \in \R^{k+2}$, the \emph{panel support
  extensor} $C(p(e)) \in \Lambda^k \R^{k+2}$ is the complement iso
  (\cref{def:meet-complement-iso}) of the grade-$2$ join $n_1 \wedge n_2 \in
  \Lambda^2 \R^{k+2}$ (\cref{def:join}).
\end{definition}

Its grade is $(k+2) - 2 = k$, the screw-space grade of
\cref{def:hinge-constraint}, so it is the supporting $k$-extensor of the
codimension-$2$ intersection of the two panels. It is nonzero exactly when the
two normals are linearly independent, the algebraic form of the two panels
meeting transversally in a nondegenerate hinge, and this is the only general-position
condition the panel constructions require.

\begin{definition}[Panel-hinge framework]
  \label{def:panel-hinge-framework}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.toBodyHinge}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-support-extensor, def:hinge-constraint}
  A \emph{panel-hinge framework} is a multigraph $G = (V,E)$ together with a
  per-body panel normal $n_v \in \R^{k+2}$, the pole of body $v$'s hyperplane
  $\mathrm{panel}(v)$, and an assignment to each edge of the ordered pair of
  bodies it joins --- its \emph{endpoint selector}. The hinge at an edge $e$
  with endpoints $(u,v)$ is the codimension-$2$ intersection
  $\mathrm{panel}(u) \cap \mathrm{panel}(v)$, with supporting $k$-extensor the
  panel support extensor $C(p(e)) = \mathrm{panelSupportExtensor}(n_u, n_v)$
  (\cref{def:panel-support-extensor}).
\end{definition}

Its \emph{body-hinge interpretation} is the body-hinge framework
(\cref{def:hinge-constraint}) on $G$ carrying exactly these supporting
extensors, so the rigidity-matrix rank theory of
\cref{sec:molecular-rigidity-matrix} applies to a panel-hinge framework
through it. Katoh--Tanigawa's body-hinge framework (KT \S2.3) requires each
hinge $p(e)$ to be a $(d-2)$-dimensional affine subspace, so
nondegeneracy is part of the definition; the framework here instead carries
the supporting extensor $C(p(e))$ as a free datum, and imposes nondegeneracy
separately where it is needed
(\cref{fmlnote:panel-hinge-nondegenerate}).

\begin{fmlnote}
  \label{fmlnote:panel-hinge-nondegenerate}
  In KT's definition (KT \S2.3) the map $p$ sends each edge to a
  $(d-2)$-dimensional affine subspace, so a hinge is nondegenerate by
  definition. Here the supporting extensor $C(p(e))$ is a free datum that may
  vanish --- a degenerate, welded hinge with $C(p(e)) = 0$ --- so
  nondegeneracy, the nonvanishing $C(p(e)) \ne 0$, appears as an explicit
  hypothesis wherever KT's definition would supply it. In particular it is a
  conjunct on both sides of the molecular characterization
  (\cref{thm:molecular-conjecture}).

  In Katoh--Tanigawa an edge carries its endpoints intrinsically; the
  formalization's multigraph keeps incidence relational, so the framework
  records the endpoint selector explicitly, a function total on the
  edge-label type $\beta$: on an edge of $G$ it selects the edge's actual
  endpoints, and on a label outside $E(G)$ it is unconstrained
  (\cref{fmlnote:endpoint-selector-totality}).
\end{fmlnote}

Every hinge incident to a body $v$ has $n_v$ as a join factor, so it lies in
the single hyperplane $\mathrm{panel}(v)$. A panel-hinge framework is therefore
hinge-coplanar by construction; a body-hinge framework is called hinge-coplanar
when it arises this way.

\begin{definition}[Hinge-coplanar body-hinge framework]
  \label{def:hinge-coplanar}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.IsHingeCoplanar,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.isHingeCoplanar_toBodyHinge}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-hinge-framework, def:hinge-constraint}
  A body-hinge framework is \emph{hinge-coplanar} when it is the body-hinge
  interpretation of a panel-hinge framework.
\end{definition}

The Case~I construction needs its hinges nondegenerate and transversal, which
the following general-position condition on the normals supplies.

\begin{definition}[Panel normals in general position]
  \label{def:panel-general-position}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.IsGeneralPosition}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-hinge-framework}
  The panel normals of a panel-hinge framework are in \emph{general position}
  when any two normals at distinct bodies are linearly independent.
\end{definition}

\begin{lemma}[General position gives nondegenerate hinges]
  \label{lem:general-position-support-nonzero}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.supportExtensor_ne_zero_of_isGeneralPosition}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-general-position, def:panel-support-extensor}
  If the panel normals are in general position, then every hinge joining two
  distinct bodies has a nonzero supporting extensor.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-support-extensor}
  The supporting extensor is the complement iso of the join of the two endpoint
  normals (\cref{def:panel-support-extensor}), which is nonzero exactly when
  those normals are linearly independent; general position supplies that
  independence at distinct bodies.
\end{proof}

This nonvanishing is the transversality the block-triangular gluing of Case~I
(\cref{lem:case-I}) requires of the hinges spanning a rigid block. A
general-position assignment exists in every dimension.

\begin{lemma}[Moment-curve normals are in general position]
  \label{lem:moment-curve-general-position}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.isGeneralPosition_withMomentNormals,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.isGeneralPosition_ofParam}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-general-position}
  A general-position assignment of panel normals exists for any number of
  bodies and any dimension.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  Set each body's normal to a point $(1, t, t^2, \dots, t^{k+1})$ on the moment
  curve at a distinct parameter $t$. Distinct parameters give distinct
  moment-curve points whose first two coordinates span a $2$-dimensional space:
  the $2 \times 2$ Vandermonde minor has determinant $t' - t \ne 0$, so any two
  normals at distinct bodies are linearly independent. An injective assignment
  of parameters gives distinct parameters at distinct bodies.
\end{proof}

\begin{fmlnote}
  Two forms are pinned: the injective-parameter form
  (\texttt{isGeneralPosition\_withMomentNormals}) and the form running directly
  from a bare graph and endpoint selector (\texttt{isGeneralPosition\_ofParam}),
  which the Case~I construction uses.
\end{fmlnote}

Standard-basis normals would cover only $|V| \le k+2$ bodies; the moment curve
places arbitrarily many.

\subsection{The generic-rank reconciliation (KT Proposition 1.1, analytic half)}
\label{sec:molecular-algebraic-induction-prop11}

KT Proposition~1.1 has two halves. The matroidal half --- the bridge
$\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = \operatorname{corank} M(\tilde G)$ between
combinatorial deficiency and matroid corank --- is \cref{thm:def-eq-corank}.
The analytic half below is the one this chapter needs: it connects the
rigidity-matrix rank of $R(G,p)$ (\cref{sec:molecular-rigidity-matrix}) to
that corank, and so identifies the graphs a generic panel realization can make
rigid. Its one nonelementary input is the generic-rank argument (KT
Claim~6.4), used throughout the induction.

\begin{proposition}[Generic rank equals $D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}$; KT Proposition 1.1, analytic half]
  \label{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.rigidityMatrix_prop11}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rigidity-matrix, def:D-deficiency, thm:def-eq-corank, def:dof-generic, lem:genericity-device}
  For a generic panel-hinge realization $(G,p)$,
  \[
    \operatorname{rank} R(G,p) = D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G).
  \]
  This rank formula is due to Jackson--Jord\'an (Theorem~6.1,
  \cite{jacksonJordan2009}).
\end{proposition}

The equation reconciles the two descriptions of rigidity used in this
development. A graph $G$ has a rigid panel-hinge realization if and only if
$\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$, that is, if and only if $\tilde G =
(D-1)\cdot G$ contains $D$ edge-disjoint spanning trees. So the rank
form of \cref{sec:molecular-rigidity-matrix} agrees with the body-hinge
existence form (\cref{thm:body-hinge-tay}).

\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55, lem:trivial-motions-rank-bound,
        lem:motions-mono-of-graph-le}
  The deficiency is the corank of $M(\tilde G)$ (\cref{thm:def-eq-corank}), so
  the claim is that a generic realization attains the maximum rank $D(|V|-1) -
  \operatorname{corank}$, equivalently that it realizes the rank hypothesis
  (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}) at $k' = \operatorname{def}$. The equality
  follows from two inequalities, one of which needs no genericity and one of
  which does.

  The upper bound $\operatorname{rank} R(G,p) \le D(|V|-1) -
  \operatorname{def}$ holds for every realization. It combines the codimension
  bound (\cref{lem:trivial-motions-rank-bound}) with a partition count: a
  partition $P$ attaining $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ contributes
  $\operatorname{def}$ independent motions beyond the $D$ trivial ones, with no
  genericity needed.

  The reverse bound $\operatorname{rank} R(G,p) \ge D(|V|-1) -
  \operatorname{def}$, equivalently $\dim Z(G,p) \le D + \operatorname{def}$,
  holds at a generic realization. Delete edges from $G$ to reach a minimal
  $k$-dof spanning subgraph, realize that subgraph at full generic rank by KT
  Theorem~5.5 (\cref{thm:theorem-55}, whose KT Claim~6.4 argument selects the
  coordinates attaining the rank), then re-add the deleted edges, which only
  grows the rank (\cref{lem:motions-mono-of-graph-le}). The remaining inputs
  are the dimension identity $n = k+1$ and the nondegenerate-hinge condition $C(e)
  \ne 0$ that the partition cut needs. This bound at every deficiency, not just
  the one a minimal spanning subgraph attains, is KT Theorem~5.6
  (\cref{thm:theorem-55-6}; $d = 3$ specialization \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-d3}).
  For a spanning $G$ ($V(G) = V_\alpha$) the $V(G)$-relative rank form of
  \cref{def:rank-hypothesis} coincides with the absolute $\dim Z = D +
  \operatorname{def}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The induction skeleton and base case}
\label{sec:molecular-algebraic-induction-base}

KT Theorem~5.5 is proved by induction on $|V(G)|$, so its statement must be
strong enough to serve as its own inductive hypothesis. That hypothesis is
KT's realization hypothesis (KT eq.~(6.1)): a minimal $k$-dof-graph has a
panel-hinge realization at the deficiency rank. We record it in two forms, a
rank form (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}) that composes through the induction and
the faithful nondegenerate-hinge form (\cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization}), then
state the two-vertex base case (\cref{lem:theorem-55-base}) and the
base-region trichotomy at which the induction terminates.

\begin{definition}[Realization hypothesis, rank form; KT eq.\ (6.1)]
  \label{def:rank-hypothesis}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.HasFullRankRealization,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.HasGenericFullRankRealization}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rigidity-matrix, def:k-dof}
  For a minimal $k$-dof-graph $G$ with $|V| \ge 2$, KT's realization
  hypothesis (KT eq.~(6.1)) asserts the existence of a panel-hinge
  realization $(G,p)$ whose rigidity rows span the maximal dimension
  \[
    \dim \operatorname{span}\bigl(\text{rigidity rows of } (G,p)\bigr)
      \;=\; D\,(|V(G)| - 1) - k.
  \]
\end{definition}

\begin{fmlnote}
  Two Lean predicates carry this hypothesis. The bare predicate
  \texttt{HasFullRankRealization} asserts only that some realization attains
  the rank; over a nonempty $V(G)$ this is the rank equality above, and it is
  what KT Theorem~5.5 establishes by induction on $|V(G)|$. It is deliberately
  weaker than KT's hypothesis, which requires nondegenerate hinges: a
  degenerate framework whose panels are all welded shut satisfies it on any
  connected $0$-dof graph, so the induction is restated in the
  nondegenerate-hinge form (\cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization}). The
  strengthened predicate \texttt{HasGenericFullRankRealization} additionally
  records that the panel normals are in general position and algebraically
  independent, the form KT Theorem~5.5 produces when $G$ is simple. The
  underlying rank-equality and rigid-on-$V(G)$ predicates the two are phrased
  against are \texttt{RankHypothesis} and \texttt{IsInfinitesimallyRigidOn}.
\end{fmlnote}

\begin{remark}[Why the target is a rank equality]
  \label{rem:rank-hypothesis-relative}
  The rigidity rows of $R(G,p)$ come only from the edges of $G$, so their span,
  and hence $\operatorname{rank} R(G,p)$, depends only on $V(G)$ and is blind to
  any ambient bodies outside $V(G)$. The target is therefore stated as a rank
  equality rather than as the null-space equality $\dim Z(G,p) = D + k$ over the
  whole screw-assignment space $\R^{D|V_\alpha|}$ of the ambient body type. That
  null space is not ambient-independent: an inductive subgraph does not span the
  ambient bodies, so its null space always carries the free motions of the
  omitted ones. Only the rank form is stable under an induction that shrinks $G$
  one vertex at a time. The two forms agree exactly when $G$ spans, the case
  used in \cref{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}. When $k = 0$ the rank equality
  reads: every infinitesimal motion is constant on $V(G)$.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Realization hypothesis, nondegenerate-hinge form; KT eq.\ (6.1)]
  \label{def:genuine-hinge-realization}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.HasPanelRealization,
    CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.ExtensorInPanel}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rank-hypothesis, def:panel-hinge-framework, def:D-deficiency}
  A graph $G$ \emph{has a nondegenerate-hinge panel realization} at dimension $k$ and
  deficiency parameter $n$ when there exist a body-hinge framework on $G$ and a
  panel-normal assignment $v \mapsto n_v \in \mathbb{R}^{k+2}$ such that: every
  body has a nonzero normal; every link's supporting $k$-extensor is nonzero and
  lies in both endpoint panels (each endpoint's hyperplane $n_v^\perp$ contains
  $k$ points spanning the extensor); and the rigidity-row span attains the
  target rank $D(|V(G)|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ (KT eq.~(6.1)).
\end{definition}

This is the realization hypothesis in the form KT actually uses. Its hinges are
nondegenerate: nonzero, transversal, and contained in the panels they support, a
condition no welded degeneracy meets.

\begin{lemma}[A generic realization is nondegenerate-hinge]
  \label{lem:generic-yields-genuine-hinge}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.hasPanelRealization_of_generic}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:genuine-hinge-realization, def:panel-general-position}
  Every generic panel-hinge framework (\cref{def:panel-general-position}) with
  $|V| \ge 2$ has a nondegenerate-hinge panel realization
  (\cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization}).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:general-position-support-nonzero}
  General position gives each link a nonzero supporting extensor
  (\cref{lem:general-position-support-nonzero}); the panel support extensor is
  the meet of the two endpoint panels, so it lies in both; and an
  infinitesimally rigid framework attains the rank count of the rank hypothesis
  at the framework's deficiency.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Realization at the target rank; KT Theorem 5.5]
  \label{thm:theorem-55}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.theorem_55_gen,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.theorem_55_minimalKDof_gen}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rank-hypothesis, thm:minimal-kdof-reduction-all-k,
        lem:theorem-55-base-producer, lem:theorem-55-base,
        lem:case-cut-edge-realization, lem:case-cut-edge-realization-gp,
        lem:case-I, lem:case-II, lem:case-I-realization, lem:case-I-dispatch,
        lem:case-II-realization, lem:case-III, lem:case-III-nested-rank-lower}
  Let $G$ be a minimal $0$-dof-graph with $|V| \ge 2$, at any dimension
  $n \ge 3$ (equivalently $D = \binom{n+1}{2} \ge 6$). Then $G$ admits a
  panel-hinge realization $(G,p)$ whose rigidity matrix attains the maximal
  rank $D(|V(G)|-1)$, i.e.\ $(G,p)$ is infinitesimally rigid on $V(G)$
  (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}, at $k' = 0$). Moreover, if $G$ is simple, the
  realization may be chosen generic: hinges in general position, with
  algebraically independent coordinates.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:minimal-kdof-reduction-all-k, lem:theorem-55-base-producer,
        lem:theorem-55-base, lem:case-cut-edge-realization,
        lem:case-cut-edge-realization-gp, lem:case-I, lem:case-II,
        lem:case-I-realization, lem:case-I-dispatch, lem:case-II-realization,
        lem:case-III, lem:case-III-nested-rank-lower, lem:chain-data-extract}
  The proof is an induction on $|V(G)|$, driven by the all-deficiency reduction
  skeleton (KT \S6, \cref{thm:minimal-kdof-reduction-all-k}): a minimal
  $k'$-dof-graph on at least three vertices is either not $2$-edge-connected, or
  has a proper rigid subgraph, or is $2$-edge-connected with no proper rigid
  subgraph, the last case split on whether its deficiency $k'$ is positive or
  zero. The base region $|V(G)| \le 2$ is realized by the trichotomy dispatch
  (\cref{lem:theorem-55-base-producer}), whose parallel-edge case is the
  two-vertex double edge (\cref{lem:theorem-55-base}).

  Each of KT's four cases is realized at the target rank. A cut edge, when $G$
  is not $2$-edge-connected, is realized by KT Lemma~6.1
  (\cref{lem:case-cut-edge-realization}, with
  \cref{lem:case-cut-edge-realization-gp} for the generic conjunct). A proper
  rigid subgraph is contracted by Case~I (KT \S6.2; \cref{lem:case-I-dispatch},
  \cref{lem:case-I-realization}). When $G$ is $2$-edge-connected with no proper
  rigid subgraph, a positive-deficiency splitting-off is realized by Case~II (KT
  Lemma~6.8; \cref{lem:case-II-realization}), and a zero-deficiency
  splitting-off, the most difficult case, by Case~III (KT \S6.4;
  \cref{lem:case-III}).

  Throughout, the induction hypothesis is quantified over \emph{all}
  deficiencies $k'$, not only $k' = 0$ (this is the strength of KT eq.~(6.1)):
  Case~III applies the statement to an auxiliary minimal $k''$-dof graph with
  $0 \le k'' \le D - 2$ (KT Claim~6.11, KT eq.~(6.22)), using the stronger
  hypothesis in its nested rank bound (\cref{lem:case-III-nested-rank-lower}).
  At general dimension Case~III proceeds by a chain-length dispatch together
  with a chain-realization argument valid for cycles of any length
  (\cref{lem:chain-data-extract}); the $d = 3$ specialization of the whole
  induction is \cref{thm:theorem-55-d3-instance}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The fresh-edge-label convention]
  \label{rem:fresh-edge-supply}
  The Lean development fixes a single ambient type of edge labels once and for
  all, a formalization convention with no analogue in Katoh--Tanigawa. The
  edge-adding surgery of the induction (splitting off a vertex, re-inserting an
  edge) needs a supply of labels not already in use. The supply is always
  available: a minimal $k$-dof-graph has at most $D(|V|-1) -
  \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ edges
  (\texttt{Graph.edgeSet\_ncard\_add\_deficiency\_le\_of\_isMinimalKDof}), so
  any edge-label type larger than that bound has a spare label
  (\texttt{Graph.freshEdgeSupply\_of\_card\_lt}). A single unconditioned supply,
  one fresh label for every graph on the label type, would not be available,
  since an all-loops-at-one-vertex graph already uses every label; this is why
  the supply is conditioned on minimality. The induction uses this per-graph
  supply at every minimal-dof-graph it visits, while the chapter's
  consumer-facing statements instead state an explicit label-headroom bound
  $D(|\alpha|-1) < |\beta|$ ($\alpha$ the body type, $\beta$ the edge-label
  type), a closed arithmetic condition a caller can check directly. This bound
  is the explicit hypothesis of \cref{thm:theorem-55},
  \cref{thm:theorem-55-d3-instance}, \cref{thm:theorem-55-6},
  \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-d3}, and \cref{thm:molecular-conjecture}.
  \Cref{rem:molecular-conjecture-nonvacuous} exhibits a concrete instance
  discharging it.
\end{remark}

\begin{fmlnote}
  The all-deficiency form \texttt{theorem\_55\_minimalKDof\_gen}, faithful to KT
  eq.~(6.1), is the statement the induction runs on; it takes the fresh-edge
  supply as a hypothesis (\cref{rem:fresh-edge-supply}). The molecular
  conjecture consumes only its $0$-dof corollary \texttt{theorem\_55\_gen}.
\end{fmlnote}

\begin{theorem}[Theorem 5.5 at $d = 3$; KT Theorem 5.5]
  \label{thm:theorem-55-d3-instance}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.theorem_55_d3}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rank-hypothesis, def:genuine-hinge-realization}
  Let $G$ be a minimal $0$-dof-graph with $|V| \ge 2$. Then $G$ admits a
  panel-hinge realization $(G,p)$ in $\R^3$ whose rigidity matrix attains
  the maximal rank $D(|V|-1)$, where $D = 6$. Moreover, if $G$ is simple,
  the realization may be chosen generic: hinges in general position, with
  algebraically independent coordinates.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55}
  Specialize KT Theorem~5.5 (\cref{thm:theorem-55}) to $n = 3$, where
  $D = \binom{4}{2} = 6$.
\end{proof}

\begin{fmlnote}
  The general-position conjunct is conditioned on simplicity: KT's nonparallel
  (generic) realizations (KT \S5.1 --- the panels of two distinct bodies meet in
  a $(d-2)$-dimensional hinge) are defined only for simple graphs, and the
  non-simple base of two parallel edges on one body pair admits none. The Lean
  statement is the conjunction of the theorem's two claims.
\end{fmlnote}

\begin{corollary}[KT Theorem 5.5 yields KT Proposition 1.1 at deficiency zero]
  \label{cor:theorem-55-d3-spanning}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.rankHypothesis_deficiency_of_theorem_55_d3}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55-d3-instance, def:rank-hypothesis}
  Let $G$ be a simple, spanning minimal $0$-dof-graph on $\ge 2$ bodies in
  $\R^3$. Then the generic realization of \cref{thm:theorem-55-d3-instance}
  already realizes the rank hypothesis (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}) at $G$'s
  own deficiency $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$: $\dim Z(G,p) = D = D +
  \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55-d3-instance, prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}
  Adjust the endpoint selector (\cref{def:panel-hinge-framework}) so that
  every edge label --- including those outside $E(G)$ --- is assigned a
  fixed pair of distinct bodies, which $|V| \ge 2$ supplies. Then every edge,
  link or not, carries a nondegenerate, transversal hinge, and the KT
  Proposition~1.1 rank count (\cref{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}) applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{fmlnote}
  \label{fmlnote:endpoint-selector-totality}
  The endpoint selector (\cref{def:panel-hinge-framework}) is a total
  function on the edge-label type $\beta$, so it assigns endpoints even to
  labels outside $E(G)$; the construction re-points those to a fixed pair of
  distinct bodies, leaving no ambient label with a degenerate hinge.
\end{fmlnote}

This is the deficiency-zero case of the strip-and-readd assembly that proves
KT Theorem~5.6 at $d = 3$ (\cref{thm:theorem-55-6-d3}): $G$ is already at the
target rank, so no stripping is needed.

KT Theorem~5.6 lifts the minimal-$0$-dof realization of KT Theorem~5.5
(\cref{thm:theorem-55}) to an arbitrary deficiency: every simple spanning
multigraph, minimal or not, attains its own deficiency rank. It is the
$\operatorname{def} > 0$ half of the KT Proposition~1.1 reconciliation
(\cref{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}), whose $\operatorname{def} = 0$ case is
KT Theorem~5.5 itself.

\begin{theorem}[Every multigraph attains the deficiency rank; KT Theorem 5.6, \S5.2]
  \label{thm:theorem-55-6}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.rankHypothesis_of_theorem_55_gen}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55, lem:motions-mono-of-graph-le,
        lem:subgraph-minimality, def:rank-hypothesis}
  Let $G$ be a simple spanning multigraph, at any dimension $n \ge 3$
  (equivalently $D = \binom{n+1}{2} \ge 6$). Then $G$ has a panel-hinge
  realization $(G,p)$ attaining the deficiency rank
  \[
    \operatorname{rank} R(G,p) = D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G),
  \]
  i.e.\ a framework realizing the rank hypothesis (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis})
  at $k' = \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55, lem:motions-mono-of-graph-le,
        lem:subgraph-minimality, prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}
  We follow Katoh--Tanigawa (p.~670), at general $d$. When $|V| = 1$ the
  framework is trivially rigid and $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$, so the
  claim is immediate.

  Otherwise, remove edges from $G$ to reach a minimal $k$-dof spanning subgraph
  $G' \le G$ with $V(G') = V(G)$ and $\operatorname{def}(\tilde{G'}) =
  \operatorname{def}(\tilde G) =: c$, a deficiency-preserving removal given by
  the matroid argument behind \cref{lem:subgraph-minimality}. Realize $G'$ at
  its own deficiency $c$ by KT Theorem~5.5 (\cref{thm:theorem-55}, at general
  dimension; $G'$ is simple as a subgraph of the simple $G$), giving a generic
  full-rank realization $q$ on $G'$ with $\dim Z(G',q) = D + c$.

  Now extend $q$ to the full graph $G$, keeping the supporting extensor on
  every $G'$-link unchanged and placing a nondegenerate in-panel hinge on each
  re-added edge; in the homogeneous model two distinct hyperplanes through the
  origin always meet, so KT's projective-invariance move is free at every
  dimension, with no new geometry beyond the $d = 3$ argument. Re-adding edges
  only shrinks the motion space (\cref{lem:motions-mono-of-graph-le}), so
  $\dim Z(G,p) \le \dim Z(G',q) = D + c = D + \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$,
  exactly the upper bound the rank hypothesis needs. Together with the
  genericity-free lower bound this
  discharges the hypothesis via the KT Proposition~1.1 rank bridge
  (\cref{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}), whose $n = k+1$ premise is the
  dimension identity $\operatorname{bodyBarDim} n = \operatorname{screwDim} k
  \Rightarrow n = k+1$.
\end{proof}

  \]
  i.e.\ a framework realizing the rank hypothesis
  (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}) at $k' = \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}

The chapter's main statement is the molecular conjecture itself. In it, a graph
is \emph{realized as an infinitesimally rigid body-hinge (or panel-hinge)
framework} when it carries a realization $(G,p)$ all of whose infinitesimal
motions are trivial and whose every hinge is \emph{nondegenerate}: its
supporting extensor satisfies $C(p(e)) \ne 0$, matching KT's requirement (KT
\S2.3) that each $p(e)$ be a $(d-2)$-dimensional affine subspace. This is the
nondegenerate-hinge form of \cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization} at deficiency
zero.

\begin{theorem}[The Molecular Conjecture: body-hinge $\Leftrightarrow$ panel-hinge rigidity; KT Conjecture 1.2]
  \label{thm:molecular-conjecture}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.molecular_conjecture}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:dof-generic, def:panel-hinge-framework}
  Let $G$ be a simple spanning graph on $\ge 2$ bodies, at any dimension
  $n \ge 3$ (equivalently $6 \le D = \binom{n+1}{2}$). Then $G$ can be realized
  as an infinitesimally rigid \emph{body-hinge} framework with nondegenerate
  hinges (\cref{fmlnote:panel-hinge-nondegenerate}) in $\R^{n}$ if and only if
  $G$ can be realized as an infinitesimally rigid \emph{panel-hinge} framework
  with nondegenerate hinges in $\R^{n}$. This is KT Conjecture~1.2, the
  \emph{Molecular Conjecture}, conjectured by Tay and
  Whiteley~\cite{tayWhiteley1984} and proved by
  Katoh--Tanigawa~\cite{katohTanigawa2011}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55-6, lem:trivial-motions-rank-bound, def:rank-hypothesis,
        def:D-deficiency}
  ($\Leftarrow$) A panel-hinge framework \emph{is} a body-hinge framework with
  the same supporting extensors (its body-hinge interpretation), so a rigid
  nondegenerate panel realization is at once a rigid nondegenerate body-hinge
  realization. This direction is elementary.

  ($\Rightarrow$) A nondegenerate body-hinge realization forces
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$. The genericity-free lower bound
  $D + \operatorname{def}(\tilde G) \le \dim Z(G,p)$
  (\cref{lem:trivial-motions-rank-bound}, which needs the nondegenerate hinges)
  meets $\dim Z(G,p) = D$ from rigidity (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis} at
  $k' = 0$), forcing $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) \le 0$, hence $= 0$.

  With $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$, KT Theorem~5.6
  (\cref{thm:theorem-55-6}, in its nondegenerate-hinge witness form) produces a
  nondegenerate panel-hinge realization at deficiency zero, i.e.\
  infinitesimally rigid. Combined with the Tay--Whiteley body-hinge theorem (KT
  Proposition~1.1: a body-hinge graph is rigid iff $\tilde G = (D-1)G$ has $D$
  edge-disjoint spanning trees, i.e.\ $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$), this
  is the full molecular characterization.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Both hypotheses are essential]
  \label{rem:molecular-conjecture-essential}
  Neither hypothesis of \cref{thm:molecular-conjecture} can be dropped. The
  nondegenerate-hinge condition is essential: a degenerate hinge $C(p(e)) = 0$
  welds its two bodies ($S(u) = S(v)$), \emph{over}-constraining the framework, so
  without it a welded framework could be infinitesimally rigid on a
  combinatorially deficient graph ($\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) > 0$) and the
  equivalence would fail.

  The $\ge 2$-body hypothesis is essential too. On a single body $E(G) =
  \emptyset$, yet the nondegenerate-hinge condition ranges over the framework's whole
  edge-label type, not just $E(G)$, so pick any label, trivially outside the
  empty $E(G)$. At that spare label the body-hinge side is still realizable: its
  supporting extensor is unconstrained, and a single unconstrained body is
  automatically rigid. The panel-hinge side is not, since a panel-hinge's hinge
  at any label is the meet of the two panel normals at its endpoints, and with
  one body every label's endpoints coincide, forcing a degenerate
  (repeated-argument) meet no matter which normals are chosen. So without the
  hypothesis the equivalence would be false, with the body-hinge side realizable
  but the panel-hinge side not, rather than vacuously true.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Non-vacuity witness]
  \label{rem:molecular-conjecture-nonvacuous}
  The label-headroom bound of \cref{thm:molecular-conjecture}
  (\cref{rem:fresh-edge-supply}) is satisfiable, so the statement is not
  vacuously true. This is checked directly: the witness
  \texttt{molecular\_conjecture\_witness} applies the theorem at a concrete
  $d = 3$ instance --- two bodies, seven edge labels, the one-edge graph ---
  discharging every hypothesis, the label-headroom bound included, with no
  \texttt{sorry}.
\end{remark}

The base of the induction is the two-vertex double edge, the minimal
$0$-dof-graph. Realized by two hinges with linearly independent supporting
extensors (KT Lemma~5.3, \cref{lem:rank-parallel-full}) it attains the full rank
$D = D(|V(G)|-1)$ at $k = 0$, which in the $V(G)$-relative rank hypothesis
(\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}) is rigidity on the two bodies.

\begin{lemma}[KT Theorem 5.5 base case ($|V(G)| = 2$)]
  \label{lem:theorem-55-base}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.theorem_55_base}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rank-hypothesis, lem:rank-parallel-full}
  Let $F$ be a body-hinge framework carrying two edges $e_1, e_2$, both joining
  the same two distinct bodies $u \ne v$, whose supporting extensors
  $C(p(e_1)), C(p(e_2))$ are linearly independent. Then every infinitesimal
  motion of $F$ is constant on $\{u, v\}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:rank-parallel-full}
  Both edges constrain the relative screw $S(u) - S(v)$ to the span of a
  supporting extensor. Their extensors being linearly independent, the
  parallel-hinges lemma (KT Lemma~5.3, \cref{lem:rank-parallel-full}) forces
  $S(u) = S(v)$ for every infinitesimal motion $S$. Every body of $\{u, v\}$ is
  $u$ or $v$, so $S$ is constant on $\{u, v\}$.
\end{proof}

The parallel-pair base case (\cref{lem:theorem-55-base-producer-parallel})
places two hinges in a single shared panel $\Pi(u) = \Pi(v) = n^{\perp}$,
carrying non-proportional supporting extensors (cf.\ KT Lemma~5.3, p.~670). The
following supplies that coincident-panel pair.

\begin{lemma}[Two independent extensors in a common panel]
  \label{lem:extensor-pair-in-panel}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.exists_linearIndependent_extensor_pair_perp}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:extensor, lem:extensor-independence}
  For any normal $n \in \R^4$ there are two point-pairs $p, q : \{0,1\} \to \R^4$, each lying
  in the panel $n^{\perp} = \{x : x \cdot n = 0\}$, whose grade-$2$ extensors
  $p_0 \wedge p_1$ and $q_0 \wedge q_1$ in $\bigwedge^2 \R^4$ are linearly independent.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:extensor-independence}
  The panel $n^{\perp}$ has dimension at least $3$ in $\R^4$ (it is the kernel of the single
  pairing functional $x \mapsto x \cdot n$, so $\dim n^{\perp} \ge 4 - 1 = 3$, with equality
  unless $n = 0$). Pick three linearly independent vectors $a, b, c \in n^{\perp}$ and set
  $p = (a, b)$, $q = (a, c)$. The two wedges $a \wedge b$ and $a \wedge c$ are linearly
  independent: a relation $s\,(a \wedge b) + t\,(a \wedge c) = 0$, joined on the left with $c$
  (resp.\ $b$), kills the second (resp.\ first) term by the alternating property and leaves
  $s\,(c \wedge a \wedge b) = 0$ (resp.\ $t\,(b \wedge a \wedge c) = 0$); the surviving triple
  wedge is nonzero by independence of $\{a, b, c\}$, so $s = t = 0$. (This is the two-subset
  instance of the join-to-isolate technique behind \cref{lem:extensor-independence}.)
\end{proof}

The base region of the induction is the minimal-dof-graphs on at most two
bodies. By the trichotomy (\cref{lem:two-vertex-trichotomy}) they fall into
three cases: the empty graph, a single edge, and a parallel edge pair. Each is
realized directly, and the dispatch (\cref{lem:theorem-55-base-producer})
assembles them.

\begin{lemma}[KT Theorem 5.5 base region ($|V(G)| \le 2$): the empty graph]
  \label{lem:theorem-55-base-producer-empty}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.theorem_55_base_producer_empty,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.theorem_55_base_producer_empty_gp}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rank-hypothesis, def:genuine-hinge-realization, lem:two-vertex-trichotomy}
  Let $G$ be a minimal-$k$-dof-graph on $1 \le |V(G)| \le 2$ bodies realizing the \emph{empty}
  case of the base trichotomy (\cref{lem:two-vertex-trichotomy} (i)): $E(G) = \emptyset$, and
  hence $\mathrm{def}(\tilde G) = D(|V(G)| - 1)$. Then $G$ has a nondegenerate-hinge panel realization
  (\cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization}) at the target rank $D(|V(G)|-1) - \mathrm{def} = 0$, and
  also a \emph{generic} full-rank realization (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}) at that same rank.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  The bare construction is geometry-free: take the all-zero framework (every edge carries the
  zero supporting extensor) at a fixed nonzero panel normal. With no edges no hinge constraint
  is imposed, so the rigidity-row family is empty, its span is $\{0\}$, and the rank is $0$; the
  per-link conjunct is vacuous. The target rank vanishes because the empty-graph
  deficiency is exactly $D(|V(G)|-1)$ (\cref{lem:two-vertex-trichotomy}). For the generic
  companion build the single-body / empty framework at an injective
  algebraically-independent seed (a non-root of the general-position polynomial): the panel
  normals are then in general position and algebraically independent, the rigidity-row family is
  still empty (rank $0$), and link-recording is vacuous.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[KT Theorem 5.5 base region ($|V(G)| \le 2$): a single edge]
  \label{lem:theorem-55-base-producer-single}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.theorem_55_base_producer_single_edge,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.theorem_55_base_producer_single_edge_gp}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rank-hypothesis, def:genuine-hinge-realization, lem:two-vertex-trichotomy,
        lem:extensor-pair-in-panel, def:rigidity-matrix}
  Let $G$ be a two-vertex minimal-$1$-dof-graph realizing the \emph{single-edge} case of the base
  trichotomy (\cref{lem:two-vertex-trichotomy} (ii)): $V(G) = \{x, y\}$ with $x \ne y$,
  $E(G) = \{e\}$ a single edge joining $x$ and $y$, and $\mathrm{def}(\tilde G) = 1$. Then $G$ has
  a nondegenerate-hinge panel realization (\cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization}) at the target rank
  $D(|V(G)|-1) - \mathrm{def} = D - 1 = 5$ (at $d = 3$, $D = 6$), and also a \emph{generic}
  full-rank realization (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}) at that same rank.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:extensor-pair-in-panel, def:rigidity-matrix}
  For the bare realization, place coincident panels $\Pi(x) = \Pi(y) = n^{\perp}$ at a fixed nonzero
  normal $n$ and assign the single hinge one nonzero supporting extensor inside the common panel
  (the first of the pair of \cref{lem:extensor-pair-in-panel}; nonzero by its linear
  independence). The lone hinge's row block already \emph{is} the entire rigidity-row family
  (there is only one edge), and that block has rank exactly $D - 1$ (\cref{def:rigidity-matrix});
  so the rigidity-row span has rank $D - 1 = D(|V(G)|-1) - \mathrm{def}$, as required.
  Unlike the parallel-pair case the framework is not rigid ($\mathrm{def} = 1$): the rank
  is the single hinge-row count, not the full two-vertex base rank. The generic companion builds
  the panel framework from a normal assignment ($\mathtt{ofNormals}$) at an injective
  algebraically-independent seed (a non-root of the
  general-position polynomial) with the single edge's two distinct endpoints: general position
  forces the lone supporting extensor nonzero, the same single hinge-row count gives rank $D - 1$,
  and the seed supplies general position $+$ algebraic independence of the panel normals.
\end{proof}

This is the one base case whose generic realization is not automatic. The
parallel-pair case carries the geometric content of the base region; the empty
and single-edge cases reduce to rank counts.

\begin{lemma}[KT Theorem 5.5 base region ($|V(G)| \le 2$): a parallel edge pair]
  \label{lem:theorem-55-base-producer-parallel}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.theorem_55_base_producer_parallel_pair}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:genuine-hinge-realization, lem:two-vertex-trichotomy, lem:theorem-55-base,
        lem:extensor-pair-in-panel}
  Let $G$ be a two-vertex minimal-$0$-dof-graph realizing the \emph{parallel-pair} case of the
  base trichotomy (\cref{lem:two-vertex-trichotomy} (iii)): $V(G) = \{x, y\}$ with $x \ne y$,
  $E(G) = \{e, f\}$ with $e \ne f$ two edges both joining $x$ and $y$, and $\mathrm{def}(\tilde G)
  = 0$. Then $G$ has a nondegenerate-hinge panel realization
  (\cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization}) at the full target rank $D(|V(G)|-1) - \mathrm{def} =
  D \cdot 1 = 6$ (at $d = 3$, $D = 6$).
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:theorem-55-base, lem:extensor-pair-in-panel}
  Place \emph{coincident panels} $\Pi(x) = \Pi(y) = n^{\perp}$ at a fixed nonzero normal
  $n$ and assign the pair's two hinges two linearly independent supporting extensors inside the
  common panel $n^{\perp}$ (\cref{lem:extensor-pair-in-panel}). Each link's supporting extensor is
  then nonzero and lies in both endpoint panels, discharging the per-link conjunct. The two
  independent extensors give the combined hinge-row blocks full rank $D$ on the relative
  screw $S(x) - S(y)$, so the two-vertex base case (\cref{lem:theorem-55-base}) makes the framework
  infinitesimally rigid on $\{x, y\} = V(G)$; the $V(G)$-relative rank bridge (rigid on $V(G)$ iff
  the rigidity-row span has rank $D(|V(G)|-1)$) turns that into the target rank equality,
  with $\mathrm{def}(\tilde G) = 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[KT Theorem 5.5 base region ($|V(G)| \le 2$): the trichotomy dispatch]
  \label{lem:theorem-55-base-producer}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.theorem_55_base_producer_gen,
        Graph.not_simple_of_isMinimalKDof_of_ncard_two}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rank-hypothesis, def:genuine-hinge-realization, lem:two-vertex-trichotomy,
        lem:theorem-55-base-producer-empty, lem:theorem-55-base-producer-single,
        lem:theorem-55-base-producer-parallel}
  Let $G$ be a minimal-$k$-dof-graph with $1 \le |V(G)| \le 2$. Then $G$ has a
  nondegenerate-hinge panel realization (\cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization}) at the
  target rank $D(|V(G)|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$, and, if $G$ is
  simple, a \emph{generic} full-rank realization (\cref{def:rank-hypothesis}) at
  that rank.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:theorem-55-base-producer-empty, lem:theorem-55-base-producer-single,
        lem:theorem-55-base-producer-parallel}
  Dispatch on the base trichotomy (\cref{lem:two-vertex-trichotomy}). The unconditional
  bare conjunct comes from the three bare cases. For the simple conjunct, the empty case
  ($E(G) = \emptyset$) and the single-edge case ($\mathrm{def} = 1 > 0$) both admit a
  \emph{generic} full-rank realization, built from a normal assignment ($\mathtt{ofNormals}$)
  at an injective algebraically-independent seed, which is a non-root of the general-position
  polynomial, so the panel normals are simultaneously in general position and algebraically
  independent; the rank is $0$ (empty) resp.\ $D - 1$ (single-edge, the single hinge-row count).
  The single-edge case is the one base case where the simple conjunct is not automatic. In the
  parallel-pair case ($k = 0$) a two-vertex minimal-$0$-dof-graph cannot be simple: the
  single-vertex cut supplies two distinct parallel edges, both forced to link the same pair,
  which simplicity collapses, a contradiction
  (\texttt{Graph.not\_simple\_of\_isMinimalKDof\_of\_ncard\_two}); the $G$-simple antecedent is
  therefore vacuous.
\end{proof}

\begin{fmlnote}
  The generic conjunct is the strengthened form of \cref{def:rank-hypothesis}
  (general position with algebraically independent panel normals); the
  unconditional conjunct is the bare nondegenerate-hinge form of
  \cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization}.
\end{fmlnote}

This conditioned pair is the base-region instance, at $|V(G)| \le 2$, of the
all-deficiency induction hypothesis KT Theorem~5.5 is proved against (the base
region of the all-$k$ reduction, \cref{thm:minimal-kdof-reduction-all-k}).

The two-body base case has a three-body analogue: a triangle with independent
supporting extensors is rigid on its three bodies.

\begin{lemma}[KT Theorem 5.5 triangle base ($|V(G)| = 3$)]
  \label{lem:theorem-55-triangle}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.theorem_55_triangle}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rank-hypothesis, lem:rank-parallel-full}
  If a body-hinge framework $F$ has three edges $e_1 : u$--$v$, $e_2 : v$--$w$,
  $e_3 : w$--$u$ forming a triangle whose three supporting extensors
  $C(p(e_1))$, $C(p(e_2))$, $C(p(e_3))$ are linearly independent, then every
  infinitesimal motion of $F$ is constant on $\{u, v, w\}$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:rank-parallel-full}
  Each hinge constraint puts a relative screw $S(u) - S(v)$, $S(v) -
  S(w)$, $S(w) - S(u)$ in the one-dimensional span of its supporting
  extensor; the three differences telescope to $0$ (the cyclic shift is a
  bijection of $\{u,v,w\}$). Linear independence of the three extensors, the
  $m = 3$ instance of \cref{lem:rank-parallel-full}'s $m$-dimensional
  generalization, forces each difference to vanish, so
  $S(u) = S(v) = S(w)$, and constancy on $\{u,v,w\}$ follows.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Cyclic-seed existence for the triangle base]
  \label{lem:triangle-normals}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.exists_triangle_normals}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-support-extensor, lem:panel-support-extensor-independence,
        lem:extensor-independence}
  When $k \ge 1$ (so $k+2 \ge 3$), there exist three vectors
  $n_0, n_1, n_2 \in \R^{k+2}$ such that (1) each cyclic pair $(n_0,n_1)$,
  $(n_1,n_2)$, $(n_2,n_0)$ has a nonzero grade-$2$ join $n_i \wedge n_j \ne 0$,
  and (2) the cyclic supporting-extensor family
  $i \mapsto \mathrm{panelSupportExtensor}(n_{\sigma(i)}, n_{\sigma(i)+1})$ is
  linearly independent.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:panel-support-extensor-independence, lem:extensor-independence}
  By \cref{lem:panel-support-extensor-independence} it suffices to show the grade-2 join
  family is linearly independent. Via \texttt{normalsJoin\_swap}, the cyclic join family
  $(e_0 \wedge e_1,\, e_1 \wedge e_2,\, e_2 \wedge e_0)$ equals (unit scalars) times the
  sorted family $(e_0 \wedge e_1,\, e_1 \wedge e_2,\, e_0 \wedge e_2)$; unit scaling
  preserves independence. Each sorted join equals the corresponding member of the
  $\Lambda^2$-basis family (\texttt{normalsJoin\_basisFun\_orderEmbOfFin}); the three
  indices $\{0,1\}$, $\{1,2\}$, $\{0,2\}$ are distinct, so the three-element subfamily
  inherits independence from the full basis family
  (\cref{lem:extensor-independence}).
\end{proof}

The triangle seed generalizes to a cycle of any length (\cref{lem:triangle-normals}
is its $m = 3$ instance). Consecutive hinges of a panel cycle share a panel, so
a panel-hinge seed assigns one normal per body, which the free extensor pairs of
\cref{lem:exists-independent-panel-extensor} do not provide.

\begin{lemma}[Cyclic-seed existence for a general cycle]
  \label{lem:cycle-normals}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.exists_cycle_normals}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-support-extensor, lem:panel-support-extensor-independence,
        lem:extensor-independence}
  For $3 \le m \le k + 2$ there exist $m$ vectors $n_0, \dots, n_{m-1} \in \R^{k+2}$
  such that (1) each cyclic pair $(n_i, n_{i+1})$ (indices mod $m$) has a nonzero
  grade-$2$ join $n_i \wedge n_{i+1} \ne 0$, and (2) the cyclic supporting-extensor
  family $i \mapsto \mathrm{panelSupportExtensor}(n_i, n_{i+1})$ is linearly
  independent.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:panel-support-extensor-independence, lem:extensor-independence}
  Witness: the standard basis restricted along the inclusion of index sets,
  $n_i = e_i$ (this is where $m \le k + 2$ enters). The $m$ cyclic joins then
  realize the $m$ distinct $2$-subsets $\{i, i+1\}$ ($i < m-1$) and $\{0, m-1\}$ ---
  the edges of the cycle $C_m$ embedded in the index set. Via the grade-2
  antisymmetry at the single wrap edge, the cyclic join family equals unit scalars
  times the sorted subfamily of the $\Lambda^2$-basis family indexed by these
  $2$-subsets; the edge-to-subset index map is injective for $m \ge 3$ (two cyclic
  edges coincide only if $2 = 0$ in $\Z/m$), so the subfamily inherits independence
  from the basis family (\cref{lem:extensor-independence}), and unit scaling
  preserves independence. Each join is nonzero since consecutive indices are
  distinct; the extensor conclusion is carried across
  \cref{lem:panel-support-extensor-independence}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The framework-construction operation}
\label{sec:molecular-algebraic-induction-withgraph}

Both inductive cases construct a realization of $G$ from a realization of a
different graph: Case~I from the contraction $G/E(H)$, Case~II from the
splitting-off $G_v^{ab}$. Both need the same operation, which keeps the hinge
assignment fixed while changing the underlying multigraph. The supporting
extensors $C(p(e))$ and hinge-row blocks depend only on the hinge data, not on
which edges $G$ carries, so only the set of edges imposing a constraint
changes. The one fact needed from the operation is the graph-monotonicity of
the motion space: deleting edges (passing to a subgraph) can only enlarge the
null space $Z(G,p)$, the direction Cases~I and~II take toward the smaller
inductive graph. On a panel realization the operation keeps the panel normals
fixed, so it preserves coplanarity.

\begin{definition}[Framework on a new graph]
  \label{def:framework-with-graph}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.withGraph,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.withGraph,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.toBodyHinge_withGraph}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:rigidity-matrix, def:panel-hinge-framework}
  For a body-hinge framework $F = (G, p)$ and any multigraph $G'$ on the same
  bodies, $F[G']$ is the framework with underlying multigraph $G'$ and the same
  hinge assignment $p$. Every supporting extensor $C(p(e))$, hinge-row block,
  and per-edge constraint is unchanged; only the set of edges imposing a
  constraint changes. The same operation applies to a panel-hinge framework
  $P$, swapping the multigraph while keeping the per-body panel normals and
  endpoint selector, and it commutes with the body-hinge interpretation,
  $(P[G'])^\flat = (P^\flat)[G']$.
\end{definition}

Case~II's $1$-extension re-inserts a body $v$ with a chosen panel, which a
second operation supplies.

\begin{definition}[Overriding one body's panel normal]
  \label{def:framework-with-normal}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.withNormal}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-hinge-framework}
  For a panel-hinge framework $P$ and a body $v$, the framework $P[v \mapsto n]$
  overrides only $v$'s panel normal, leaving the multigraph, endpoint selector,
  and every other normal fixed.
\end{definition}

Each hinge whose endpoints avoid $v$ keeps its supporting extensor. Before $v$'s
two new edges are added it is unhinged, as in the splitting-off $G_v^{ab}$, so
overriding its normal leaves the inductive realization untouched away from $v$
and preserves its motion count.

\begin{lemma}[Overriding an unhinged body preserves the motion count]
  \label{lem:with-normal-preserves}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.toBodyHinge_withNormal_supportExtensor_of_ne,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.toBodyHinge_withNormal_infinitesimalMotions_eq,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.toBodyHinge_withNormal_pinnedMotions_eq}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:framework-with-normal}
  When $v$ is unhinged, meaning no edge of $G$ has $v$ as an endpoint, overriding
  $v$'s panel normal changes neither the null space $Z(G,p)$ nor any
  body-$w$-pinned motion subspace.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:framework-with-normal}
  A hinge whose endpoints avoid $v$ keeps its supporting extensor
  (\texttt{toBodyHinge\_withNormal\_supportExtensor\_of\_ne}), and when $v$ is
  unhinged every hinge avoids $v$; so the hinge-row family, and hence the null
  space and every pinned motion subspace, is unchanged
  (\texttt{toBodyHinge\_withNormal\_infinitesimalMotions\_eq},
  \texttt{toBodyHinge\_withNormal\_pinnedMotions\_eq}).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Edge deletion enlarges the motion space]
  \label{lem:motions-mono-of-graph-le}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.infinitesimalMotions_le_withGraph_of_le,
        CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.finrank_infinitesimalMotions_le_of_graph_le}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:framework-with-graph}
  If $G' \le G$ then $Z(G,p) \subseteq Z(G', p)$, equivalently
  $\dim Z(G,p) \le \dim Z(G', p)$, i.e.\
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G', p) \le \operatorname{rank} R(G, p)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:framework-with-graph}
  Each link of $G'$ is a link of $G$ (\texttt{Graph.IsLink.mono}), and
  the matching supporting extensors agree, so every infinitesimal motion
  of $G$ meets every constraint of $G'$: $Z(G,p) \subseteq Z(G', p)$.
  Taking dimensions (\texttt{Submodule.finrank\_mono}) gives the rank
  form.
\end{proof}

This is the ``re-adding edges only grows the rank'' monotonicity that lifts a
realization of a minimal $k$-dof spanning subgraph to the whole multigraph, the
step \cref{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11} uses (cf.\ the fixed-graph
span-monotonicity of KT Lemma~5.2).
