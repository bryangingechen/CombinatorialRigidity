\section{The generic form of the realization theorems}
\label{sec:generic-lift}

The realization theorems of the preceding chapters are existence
statements. Tay's theorem (\cref{thm:tay-witness}) and the body-hinge
Tay--Whiteley theorem (\cref{thm:body-hinge-tay}) construct a single
independent (resp.\ infinitesimally rigid) realization exactly when
the tree-packing count holds, and the molecule rank formula
(\cref{cor:molecule-rank-formula}) computes the rank of the generic
bar-joint matroid rather than the rank of any one realization. The
classical statements are stronger: \emph{almost all} realizations
attain the generic rank. This chapter proves that stronger form,
following the coordinate approach of Jackson and
Jord\'an~\cite{jacksonJordan2009}, which avoids the
irreducible-variety argument of
Whiteley~\cite[Proposition~6]{whiteley1988} that the body-bar and
body-hinge chapters left aside.

The pattern is uniform across the framework classes, and is the one
already in place for bar-joint placements
(\cref{sec:bar-joint-3d-generic-placement}). A realization is
\emph{generic} when every family of rigidity-matrix rows that is
linearly independent at some realization is linearly independent at
it (\cref{def:generic-placement} is the bar-joint instance). Such a
realization is in particular generic in the sense of Jackson and
Jord\'an --- its rigidity matrix and all edge-induced submatrices
attain their maximum rank over all
realizations~\cite[\S5]{jacksonJordan2009} --- and for classes with
one rigidity row per edge the two conditions coincide. Every
existence theorem then upgrades at once: a generic realization has
rank at least that of any witness realization, so a rank attained
once is attained at every generic realization.

Two statements quantify how common generic realizations are. They
exist (\cref{lem:exists-generic-placement}); and, since the entries
of the rigidity matrix are polynomials in the realization parameters,
the non-generic realizations are confined to the zero set of a single
nonzero polynomial (\cref{lem:generic-placement-abundance}). Over
$\R$ the complement of that zero set is dense and open, which is the
precise content of ``almost all realizations are generic''; Jackson
and Jord\'an instead note that realizations whose coordinates are
algebraically independent over $\Q$ are
generic~\cite[\S5]{jacksonJordan2009}.

The molecular form comes first (\cref{sec:generic-lift-molecule}):
every generic bar-joint realization of a square $G^2$ realizes the
molecule rank formula, and in particular is infinitesimally rigid
whenever $5 \cdot G$ contains six edge-disjoint spanning trees. The
panel-and-hinge form, over the panel normals, comes second
(\cref{sec:generic-lift-panel}); the body-bar form (over the bar
endpoints) and the body-and-hinge form (over points spanning the
hinges) follow the same pattern over their own realization
parameters.

\subsection{Abundance of generic placements}
\label{sec:generic-lift-abundance}

The existence lemma (\cref{lem:exists-generic-placement})
interpolates along one line at a time. The abundance form
strengthens it: one polynomial witnesses genericity across the whole
placement space.

\begin{lemma}[Abundance of generic placements]
  \label{lem:generic-placement-abundance}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.exists_isGenericPlacement_abundance}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-placement}
  Let $V$ be finite and $d$ a dimension. There is a nonzero real
  polynomial $P$ in the $d\,|V|$ coordinates of a placement such that
  every placement $p : V \to \R^d$ with $P(p) \ne 0$ is generic for
  row independence. In particular the non-generic placements are
  contained in the zero set of a nonzero polynomial.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:edgeSet-rowIndependent}
  For an edge subset $I \subseteq E(K_V)$ that is row-independent at
  some placement $q_I$, the rigidity rows indexed by $I$ are linear in
  the placement coordinates, so a maximal square minor of the row
  family, chosen nonsingular at $q_I$, is a nonzero polynomial $P_I$ in
  the placement coordinates whose non-vanishing at a placement is
  sufficient for $I$ to remain row-independent there. Let $P$ be the
  product of the $P_I$ over
  the finitely many such subsets $I$; a finite product of nonzero
  polynomials is nonzero. If $P(p) \ne 0$ then every factor
  $P_I(p) \ne 0$, so every edge subset row-independent at some
  placement is row-independent at $p$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Every generic realization of a square}
\label{sec:generic-lift-molecule}

At a placement generic for row independence the realized rank of
every graph equals its generic rank
(\cref{lem:rigidityMap-rank-at-generic}), so the molecule rank
formula (\cref{cor:molecule-rank-formula}) --- a statement about the
generic matroid --- becomes a statement about every generic
realization of the square.

\begin{theorem}[The molecule rank formula at every generic placement]
  \label{thm:molecule-generic-rank}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.molecule_generic_rank}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-placement, def:square-graph, def:D-deficiency,
    def:rigidityMap}
  Let $G$ be a simple graph of minimum degree at least two on a
  finite, nonempty vertex set $V$, and let $p : V \to \R^3$ be a
  placement generic for row independence. Then
  \[
    \operatorname{rank} R(G^2, p)
      \;=\; 3|V| - 6 - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G),
  \]
  where $\tilde G = 5 \cdot G$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{cor:molecule-rank-formula, lem:rigidityMap-rank-at-generic}
  At a generic placement the rank of the rigidity matrix of $G^2$
  equals the generic rank $r(G^2)$
  (\cref{lem:rigidityMap-rank-at-generic}), and
  $r(G^2) = 3|V| - 6 - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ is
  \cref{cor:molecule-rank-formula}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Every generic realization of a rigid square]
  \label{cor:molecule-generic-rigid}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.molecule_generic_rigid}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-placement, def:square-graph, def:D-deficiency,
    def:isInfinitesimallyRigid}
  Let $G$ be a simple graph of minimum degree at least two on a
  finite, nonempty vertex set $V$, and suppose
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$ for $\tilde G = 5 \cdot G$. Then
  for every placement $p : V \to \R^3$ generic for row independence
  the framework $(G^2, p)$ is infinitesimally rigid.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:molecule-generic-rank}
  By \cref{thm:molecule-generic-rank},
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G^2, p) = 3|V| - 6$, so by rank--nullity in
  the $3|V|$-dimensional space of motions
  $\dim \ker R(G^2, p) = 6$, the bound of
  \cref{def:isInfinitesimallyRigid}.
\end{proof}

Jackson and Jord\'an observed~\cite[p.~586]{jacksonJordan2009} that
the molecular conjecture, then open, would imply that every generic
bar-joint realization of $G^2$ in $\R^3$ is infinitesimally rigid
whenever $5 \cdot G$ contains six edge-disjoint spanning trees. The
conjecture is a theorem of this development
(\cref{thm:molecular-conjecture}), so the implication holds
unconditionally.

\begin{corollary}[Generic rigidity of squares from six spanning
  trees; Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{cor:molecule-generic-square-packing}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.molecule_generic_square_packing}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-placement, def:square-graph,
    def:isInfinitesimallyRigid}
  Let $G$ be a simple graph on a finite, nonempty vertex set $V$, and
  suppose $5 \cdot G$ contains six edge-disjoint spanning trees. Then
  every bar-joint realization of $G^2$ in $\R^3$ at a placement
  generic for row independence is infinitesimally rigid.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{cor:molecule-generic-rigid, thm:def-eq-corank, def:matroid-MG,
    def:cut-edges-2ec}
  For $|V| = 1$ the square has no edges and every placement is
  infinitesimally rigid ($\dim \ker R(G^2, p) \le 3 \le 6$,
  independently of the packing hypothesis). For $|V| \ge 2$: each of
  the six spanning trees has exactly $|V| - 1$ edges and, being
  acyclic, is independent in the cycle matroid of $5 \cdot G$; the six
  trees being pairwise edge-disjoint, their union is an independent
  set of size $6(|V| - 1)$ in the sixfold cycle-matroid union, hence
  in $M(\tilde G)$ (\cref{def:matroid-MG}). Since
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) \ge 0$ caps
  $\operatorname{rank} M(\tilde G) \le 6(|V| - 1)$, this independent
  set is a base, so $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$ by
  \cref{thm:def-eq-corank}. A $0$-dof graph is $2$-edge-connected
  (\cref{def:cut-edges-2ec}), so every vertex of the shadowing
  carrier has degree at least two, and its degree matches $G$'s
  one-for-one, so every vertex of $G$ has degree at least two;
  \cref{cor:molecule-generic-rigid} applies.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Every generic panel-and-hinge realization}
\label{sec:generic-lift-panel}

A panel-and-hinge realization of a multigraph assigns each body a
hyperplane and each edge the codimension-two intersection of the
hyperplanes at its two endpoints. In the homogeneous model of
\cref{def:panel-hinge-framework} a hyperplane is recorded by its
normal $n_v \in K^{k+2}$, where $d = k+1$ is the dimension and
$D = \binom{d+1}{2}$, so the realization parameters form the
$(k+2)\,|V|$-coordinate space of normal assignments --- the panel
analogue of Jackson and Jord\'an's map sending each vertex to the
pole of its panel hyperplane~\cite[\S7]{jacksonJordan2009}. Since a
multigraph's edges carry their endpoints only relationally, the row
family below is indexed through a fixed \emph{endpoint selector}
assigning each edge label an ordered pair of bodies; a selector is
\emph{link-recording} when the assigned pair are actual endpoints of
the edge.

The rigidity matrix of the framework induced by a normal assignment
has one block of rows per edge, spanning the $(D-1)$-dimensional
annihilator of the edge's supporting extensor line and indexed by
pairs of grade-$k$ exterior basis indices, and each row coordinate
is a polynomial of degree two in the normal coordinates
(\cref{lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}). Genericity takes the same
transfer form as for bar-joint placements
(\cref{def:generic-placement}); as there, a generic normal
assignment attains the maximum rank of the rigidity matrix and of
every edge-induced submatrix, which is Jackson and Jord\'an's
definition of a generic panel-and-hinge
realization~\cite[\S7]{jacksonJordan2009}.

Jackson and Jord\'an prove their Theorem~7.2 by perturbing the
hinge-coplanar body-and-hinge realization of their Theorem~6.5 into
a non-degenerate one (their Lemma~7.1), which is then induced by a
panel-and-hinge realization. That detour is not needed here: the
realization supplied by \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine} is already a
panel-and-hinge framework attaining the deficiency rank with every
hinge nondegenerate.

\begin{definition}[Generic normal assignment]
  \label{def:generic-normals}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.normalRow,
    CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.IsGenericNormals}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-hinge-framework, def:rigidity-matrix}
  Fix finite body and edge label sets and an endpoint selector. For
  a normal assignment $q$, the \emph{annihilator-row family} of $q$
  is the family of rigidity rows of the panel-hinge framework whose
  panel normals are read off $q$, indexed by an edge label together
  with a pair of grade-$k$ exterior basis indices
  (\cref{lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}). The assignment $q$ is
  \emph{generic for row independence} if every subfamily of the
  annihilator-row family that is linearly independent at some normal
  assignment is linearly independent at $q$.
\end{definition}

The annihilator-row family reads only the endpoint selector and the
normal assignment, not a graph, so genericity is a property of the
assignment alone; the statements below instantiate it over a
multigraph each of whose edge labels links its selector pair.

\begin{lemma}[Abundance of generic normal assignments]
  \label{lem:generic-normals-abundance}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.exists_isGenericNormals_abundance}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-normals}
  There is a nonzero polynomial $P$ in the $(k+2)\,|V|$ normal
  coordinates such that every normal assignment $q$ with
  $P(q) \ne 0$ is generic for row independence. In particular the
  non-generic assignments are contained in the zero set of a nonzero
  polynomial.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}
  For a subfamily $s$ of row indices that is linearly independent at
  some assignment $q_s$, the coordinates of the rows are degree-two
  polynomials in the normal coordinates
  (\cref{lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}), so a maximal square minor
  of the coordinate matrix, chosen nonsingular at $q_s$, is a
  nonzero polynomial $P_s$ whose non-vanishing at an assignment
  suffices for $s$ to remain independent there --- the witnessing
  minor of \cref{lem:generic-placement-abundance}'s proof, the rows
  now quadratic rather than linear in the parameters, a difference
  the argument never uses. Let $P$ be the product of the $P_s$ over
  the finitely many subfamilies independent at some assignment; a
  finite product of nonzero polynomials is nonzero.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Existence of generic normal assignments]
  \label{lem:exists-generic-normals}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.exists_isGenericNormals}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-normals}
  Over an infinite field, generic normal assignments exist.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:generic-normals-abundance}
  A nonzero polynomial over an infinite field does not vanish
  identically, so the polynomial of
  \cref{lem:generic-normals-abundance} has a non-root, which is a
  generic assignment.
\end{proof}

Existence here needs no interpolation along lines, in contrast to
\cref{lem:exists-generic-placement}: the abundance polynomial
supplies it directly.

\begin{lemma}[Generic normal assignments have nondegenerate hinges]
  \label{lem:generic-normals-nondegenerate}
  \uses{def:generic-normals, def:panel-hinge-framework,
    def:panel-support-extensor}
  Let $K$ be infinite and the dimension at least two (so
  $D \ge 3$), and let $G$ be a loopless multigraph each of whose
  edge labels links its selector pair. Then at every normal
  assignment $q$ generic for row independence, the induced framework
  on $G$ has every supporting extensor nonzero.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{lem:general-position-support-nonzero,
    lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}
  At a seed assignment placing the bodies at distinct points of the
  moment curve, any two normals are linearly independent, so every
  supporting extensor is nonzero
  (\cref{lem:general-position-support-nonzero}; the endpoints of
  each edge are distinct since $G$ is loopless). A nonzero extensor
  has a nonzero annihilator row, its row block spanning the
  $(D-1)$-dimensional annihilator of the extensor line with
  $D \ge 3$; a one-row subfamily nonzero at the seed is linearly
  independent there, hence linearly independent --- nonzero --- at
  $q$. The rows are linear in the supporting extensor, so a nonzero
  row at $q$ forces the extensor at $q$ to be nonzero.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[A witness realization transplants into the normal
  coordinates]
  \label{lem:panel-witness-transplant}
  \uses{def:panel-hinge-framework, def:rigidity-matrix,
    def:panel-support-extensor}
  Let $G$ be a multigraph each of whose edge labels links its
  selector pair, and let $(G,p)$ be a panel-hinge realization of $G$
  with every supporting extensor nonzero whose own endpoint selector
  also records a link of every edge. Then for every
  $N \le \operatorname{rank} R(G,p)$ there are $N$ indices of the
  annihilator-row family that are linearly independent at the normal
  assignment reading off $(G,p)$'s panel normals.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}
  Under nonzero extensors the annihilator rows over the fixed
  selector span the row space of $R(G,p)$, so an independent
  subfamily of $N$ actual rows of $(G,p)$ can be extracted and
  re-indexed by edge and basis pair, as in
  \cref{lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract} (in the rank-input
  form discussed after that lemma, consuming the rank bound rather
  than rigidity). At each edge the realization's selector and the
  fixed selector record the same unordered pair of endpoints, and
  swapping the two panels negates their meet
  (\cref{def:panel-support-extensor}), so each extracted row agrees
  up to sign with the corresponding member of the annihilator-row
  family at the read-off assignment; linear independence is
  unchanged by signs.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[The deficiency rank at every generic normal
  assignment]
  \label{thm:panel-generic-rank}
  \uses{def:generic-normals, def:panel-hinge-framework,
    def:rank-hypothesis, def:D-deficiency}
  Let $G$ be a simple spanning multigraph on at least two bodies, at
  any dimension $n \ge 3$ (equivalently $D = \binom{n+1}{2} \ge 6$)
  and over any infinite field $K$, with a link-recording endpoint
  selector. Then every normal assignment $q$ generic for row
  independence attains the deficiency rank:
  \[
    \operatorname{rank} R(G,q)
      \;=\; D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G).
  \]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine, lem:panel-witness-transplant,
    lem:generic-normals-nondegenerate, prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}
  For the lower bound: the link-recording nondegenerate witness of
  \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine} attains
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G,p) = D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde
  G)$, so by \cref{lem:panel-witness-transplant} that many indices
  of the annihilator-row family are linearly independent at one
  normal assignment, hence at $q$ (\cref{def:generic-normals}), and
  the annihilator rows of a link-recording selector are rows of
  $R(G,q)$. The upper bound is the genericity-free bound
  $\dim Z(G,q) \ge D + \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ of
  \cref{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}, whose nondegenerate-hinge
  hypothesis holds at $q$ by
  \cref{lem:generic-normals-nondegenerate}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Every generic panel-and-hinge realization of a
  rigid graph; Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{cor:panel-generic-rigid}
  \uses{def:generic-normals, def:panel-hinge-framework,
    def:D-deficiency}
  In the setting of \cref{thm:panel-generic-rank}, the framework at
  every normal assignment generic for row independence is
  infinitesimally rigid on $V(G)$ if and only if
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{thm:panel-generic-rank, lem:exists-generic-normals,
    lem:isInfRigidOn-of-relative-count,
    lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}
  If $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$, then at a generic $q$
  \cref{thm:panel-generic-rank} gives
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G,q) = D(|V|-1)$, so
  $\dim Z(G,q) = D|V| - D(|V|-1) = D$ (the graph is spanning) and
  the framework is rigid on $V(G)$
  (\cref{lem:isInfRigidOn-of-relative-count}). Conversely a generic
  assignment exists over the infinite field
  (\cref{lem:exists-generic-normals}); rigidity on $V(G)$ there
  forces $\operatorname{rank} R(G,q) = D(|V|-1)$ (the rank--nullity
  count of \cref{lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}), which
  compared with \cref{thm:panel-generic-rank} gives
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$.
\end{proof}

This is the sharp form of Jackson and Jord\'an's
Theorem~7.2~\cite[\S7]{jacksonJordan2009}: their sufficient
condition --- $d$ edge-disjoint spanning trees in $(d-1)G$ ---
implies $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$, by repeating one further
spanning tree of $G$ over the remaining multiplicities to produce
$D$ edge-disjoint spanning trees in $(D-1)G$, as in their
Theorem~6.5. The tree-packing reformulation of the deficiency-zero
condition is taken up with the body-and-hinge form of the lift.
