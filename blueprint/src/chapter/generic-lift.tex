\section{The generic form of the realization theorems}
\label{sec:generic-lift}

The realization theorems of the preceding chapters are existence
statements. Tay's theorem (\cref{thm:tay-witness}) and the body-hinge
Tay--Whiteley theorem (\cref{thm:body-hinge-tay}) construct a single
independent (resp.\ infinitesimally rigid) realization exactly when
the tree-packing count holds, and the molecule rank formula
(\cref{cor:molecule-rank-formula}) computes the rank of the generic
bar-joint matroid rather than the rank of any one realization. The
classical statements are stronger: \emph{almost all} realizations
attain the generic rank. This chapter proves that stronger form,
following the coordinate approach of Jackson and
Jord\'an~\cite{jacksonJordan2009}, which avoids the
irreducible-variety argument of
Whiteley~\cite[Proposition~6]{whiteley1988} that the body-bar and
body-hinge chapters left aside.

The pattern is uniform across the framework classes, and is the one
already in place for bar-joint placements
(\cref{sec:bar-joint-3d-generic-placement}). A realization is
\emph{generic} when every family of rigidity-matrix rows that is
linearly independent at some realization is linearly independent at
it (\cref{def:generic-placement} is the bar-joint instance). Such a
realization is in particular generic in the sense of Jackson and
Jord\'an --- its rigidity matrix and all edge-induced submatrices
attain their maximum rank over all
realizations~\cite[\S5]{jacksonJordan2009} --- and for classes with
one rigidity row per edge the two conditions coincide. Every
existence theorem then upgrades at once: a generic realization has
rank at least that of any witness realization, so a rank attained
once is attained at every generic realization.

Two statements quantify how common generic realizations are. They
exist (\cref{lem:exists-generic-placement}); and, since the entries
of the rigidity matrix are polynomials in the realization parameters,
the non-generic realizations are confined to the zero set of a single
nonzero polynomial (\cref{lem:generic-placement-abundance}). Over
$\R$ the complement of that zero set is dense and open, which is the
precise content of ``almost all realizations are generic''; Jackson
and Jord\'an instead note that realizations whose coordinates are
algebraically independent over $\Q$ are
generic~\cite[\S5]{jacksonJordan2009}.

The molecular form comes first (\cref{sec:generic-lift-molecule}):
every generic bar-joint realization of a square $G^2$ realizes the
molecule rank formula, and in particular is infinitesimally rigid
whenever $5 \cdot G$ contains six edge-disjoint spanning trees. The
panel-and-hinge form, over the panel normals, comes second
(\cref{sec:generic-lift-panel}); the body-and-bar form, over the
bar endpoints, comes third (\cref{sec:generic-lift-bodybar}); the
body-and-hinge form, over points spanning the hinges, completes the
pattern (\cref{sec:generic-lift-bodyhinge}).

\subsection{Abundance of generic placements}
\label{sec:generic-lift-abundance}

The existence lemma (\cref{lem:exists-generic-placement})
interpolates along one line at a time. The abundance form
strengthens it: one polynomial witnesses genericity across the whole
placement space.

\begin{lemma}[Abundance of generic placements]
  \label{lem:generic-placement-abundance}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.exists_isGenericPlacement_abundance}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-placement}
  Let $V$ be finite and $d$ a dimension. There is a nonzero real
  polynomial $P$ in the $d\,|V|$ coordinates of a placement such that
  every placement $p : V \to \R^d$ with $P(p) \ne 0$ is generic for
  row independence. In particular the non-generic placements are
  contained in the zero set of a nonzero polynomial.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:edgeSet-rowIndependent}
  For an edge subset $I \subseteq E(K_V)$ that is row-independent at
  some placement $q_I$, the rigidity rows indexed by $I$ are linear in
  the placement coordinates, so a maximal square minor of the row
  family, chosen nonsingular at $q_I$, is a nonzero polynomial $P_I$ in
  the placement coordinates whose non-vanishing at a placement is
  sufficient for $I$ to remain row-independent there. Let $P$ be the
  product of the $P_I$ over
  the finitely many such subsets $I$; a finite product of nonzero
  polynomials is nonzero. If $P(p) \ne 0$ then every factor
  $P_I(p) \ne 0$, so every edge subset row-independent at some
  placement is row-independent at $p$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Every generic realization of a square}
\label{sec:generic-lift-molecule}

At a placement generic for row independence the realized rank of
every graph equals its generic rank
(\cref{lem:rigidityMap-rank-at-generic}), so the molecule rank
formula (\cref{cor:molecule-rank-formula}) --- a statement about the
generic matroid --- becomes a statement about every generic
realization of the square.

\begin{theorem}[The molecule rank formula at every generic placement]
  \label{thm:molecule-generic-rank}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.molecule_generic_rank}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-placement, def:square-graph, def:D-deficiency,
    def:rigidityMap}
  Let $G$ be a simple graph of minimum degree at least two on a
  finite, nonempty vertex set $V$, and let $p : V \to \R^3$ be a
  placement generic for row independence. Then
  \[
    \operatorname{rank} R(G^2, p)
      \;=\; 3|V| - 6 - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G),
  \]
  where $\tilde G = 5 \cdot G$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{cor:molecule-rank-formula, lem:rigidityMap-rank-at-generic}
  At a generic placement the rank of the rigidity matrix of $G^2$
  equals the generic rank $r(G^2)$
  (\cref{lem:rigidityMap-rank-at-generic}), and
  $r(G^2) = 3|V| - 6 - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ is
  \cref{cor:molecule-rank-formula}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Every generic realization of a rigid square]
  \label{cor:molecule-generic-rigid}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.molecule_generic_rigid}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-placement, def:square-graph, def:D-deficiency,
    def:isInfinitesimallyRigid}
  Let $G$ be a simple graph of minimum degree at least two on a
  finite, nonempty vertex set $V$, and suppose
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$ for $\tilde G = 5 \cdot G$. Then
  for every placement $p : V \to \R^3$ generic for row independence
  the framework $(G^2, p)$ is infinitesimally rigid.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:molecule-generic-rank}
  By \cref{thm:molecule-generic-rank},
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G^2, p) = 3|V| - 6$, so by rank--nullity in
  the $3|V|$-dimensional space of motions
  $\dim \ker R(G^2, p) = 6$, the bound of
  \cref{def:isInfinitesimallyRigid}.
\end{proof}

Jackson and Jord\'an observed~\cite[p.~586]{jacksonJordan2009} that
the molecular conjecture, then open, would imply that every generic
bar-joint realization of $G^2$ in $\R^3$ is infinitesimally rigid
whenever $5 \cdot G$ contains six edge-disjoint spanning trees. The
conjecture is a theorem of this development
(\cref{thm:molecular-conjecture}), so the implication holds
unconditionally.

\begin{corollary}[Generic rigidity of squares from six spanning
  trees; Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{cor:molecule-generic-square-packing}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.molecule_generic_square_packing}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-placement, def:square-graph,
    def:isInfinitesimallyRigid}
  Let $G$ be a simple graph on a finite, nonempty vertex set $V$, and
  suppose $5 \cdot G$ contains six edge-disjoint spanning trees. Then
  every bar-joint realization of $G^2$ in $\R^3$ at a placement
  generic for row independence is infinitesimally rigid.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{cor:molecule-generic-rigid, thm:def-eq-corank, def:matroid-MG,
    def:cut-edges-2ec}
  For $|V| = 1$ the square has no edges and every placement is
  infinitesimally rigid ($\dim \ker R(G^2, p) \le 3 \le 6$,
  independently of the packing hypothesis). For $|V| \ge 2$: each of
  the six spanning trees has exactly $|V| - 1$ edges and, being
  acyclic, is independent in the cycle matroid of $5 \cdot G$; the six
  trees being pairwise edge-disjoint, their union is an independent
  set of size $6(|V| - 1)$ in the sixfold cycle-matroid union, hence
  in $M(\tilde G)$ (\cref{def:matroid-MG}). Since
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) \ge 0$ caps
  $\operatorname{rank} M(\tilde G) \le 6(|V| - 1)$, this independent
  set is a base, so $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$ by
  \cref{thm:def-eq-corank}. A $0$-dof graph is $2$-edge-connected
  (\cref{def:cut-edges-2ec}), so every vertex of the shadowing
  carrier has degree at least two, and its degree matches $G$'s
  one-for-one, so every vertex of $G$ has degree at least two;
  \cref{cor:molecule-generic-rigid} applies.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Every generic panel-and-hinge realization}
\label{sec:generic-lift-panel}

A panel-and-hinge realization of a multigraph assigns each body a
hyperplane and each edge the codimension-two intersection of the
hyperplanes at its two endpoints. In the homogeneous model of
\cref{def:panel-hinge-framework} a hyperplane is recorded by its
normal $n_v \in K^{k+2}$, where $d = k+1$ is the dimension and
$D = \binom{d+1}{2}$, so the realization parameters form the
$(k+2)\,|V|$-coordinate space of normal assignments --- the panel
analogue of Jackson and Jord\'an's map sending each vertex to the
pole of its panel hyperplane~\cite[\S7]{jacksonJordan2009}. Since a
multigraph's edges carry their endpoints only relationally, the row
family below is indexed through a fixed \emph{endpoint selector}
assigning each edge label an ordered pair of bodies; a selector is
\emph{link-recording} when the assigned pair are actual endpoints of
the edge.

The rigidity matrix of the framework induced by a normal assignment
has one block of rows per edge, spanning the $(D-1)$-dimensional
annihilator of the edge's supporting extensor line and indexed by
pairs of grade-$k$ exterior basis indices, and each row coordinate
is a polynomial of degree two in the normal coordinates
(\cref{lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}). Genericity takes the same
transfer form as for bar-joint placements
(\cref{def:generic-placement}); as there, a generic normal
assignment attains the maximum rank of the rigidity matrix and of
every edge-induced submatrix, which is Jackson and Jord\'an's
definition of a generic panel-and-hinge
realization~\cite[\S7]{jacksonJordan2009}.

Jackson and Jord\'an prove their Theorem~7.2 by perturbing the
hinge-coplanar body-and-hinge realization of their Theorem~6.5 into
a non-degenerate one (their Lemma~7.1), which is then induced by a
panel-and-hinge realization. That detour is not needed here: the
realization supplied by \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine} is already a
panel-and-hinge framework attaining the deficiency rank with every
hinge nondegenerate.

\begin{definition}[Generic normal assignment]
  \label{def:generic-normals}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.normalRow,
    CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.IsGenericNormals}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-hinge-framework, def:rigidity-matrix}
  Fix finite body and edge label sets and an endpoint selector. For
  a normal assignment $q$, the \emph{annihilator-row family} of $q$
  is the family of rigidity rows of the panel-hinge framework whose
  panel normals are read off $q$, indexed by an edge label together
  with a pair of grade-$k$ exterior basis indices
  (\cref{lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}). The assignment $q$ is
  \emph{generic for row independence} if every subfamily of the
  annihilator-row family that is linearly independent at some normal
  assignment is linearly independent at $q$.
\end{definition}

The annihilator-row family reads only the endpoint selector and the
normal assignment, not a graph, so genericity is a property of the
assignment alone; the statements below instantiate it over a
multigraph each of whose edge labels links its selector pair.

\begin{lemma}[Abundance of generic normal assignments]
  \label{lem:generic-normals-abundance}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.exists_isGenericNormals_abundance}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-normals}
  There is a nonzero polynomial $P$ in the $(k+2)\,|V|$ normal
  coordinates such that every normal assignment $q$ with
  $P(q) \ne 0$ is generic for row independence. In particular the
  non-generic assignments are contained in the zero set of a nonzero
  polynomial.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}
  For a subfamily $s$ of row indices that is linearly independent at
  some assignment $q_s$, the coordinates of the rows are degree-two
  polynomials in the normal coordinates
  (\cref{lem:rows-polynomial-in-normals}), so a maximal square minor
  of the coordinate matrix, chosen nonsingular at $q_s$, is a
  nonzero polynomial $P_s$ whose non-vanishing at an assignment
  suffices for $s$ to remain independent there --- the witnessing
  minor of \cref{lem:generic-placement-abundance}'s proof, the rows
  now quadratic rather than linear in the parameters, a difference
  the argument never uses. Let $P$ be the product of the $P_s$ over
  the finitely many subfamilies independent at some assignment; a
  finite product of nonzero polynomials is nonzero.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Existence of generic normal assignments]
  \label{lem:exists-generic-normals}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.exists_isGenericNormals}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-normals}
  Over an infinite field, generic normal assignments exist.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:generic-normals-abundance}
  A nonzero polynomial over an infinite field does not vanish
  identically, so the polynomial of
  \cref{lem:generic-normals-abundance} has a non-root, which is a
  generic assignment.
\end{proof}

Existence here needs no interpolation along lines, in contrast to
\cref{lem:exists-generic-placement}: the abundance polynomial
supplies it directly.

\begin{lemma}[Generic normal assignments have nondegenerate hinges]
  \label{lem:generic-normals-nondegenerate}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.supportExtensor_ofNormals_ne_zero_of_isGenericNormals}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-normals, def:panel-hinge-framework,
    def:panel-support-extensor}
  Let $k \ge 1$ (so $D \ge 2$; since $D = \binom{k+2}{2}$ takes only
  the values $1, 3, 6, 10, \dots$, $D \ge 2$ and $D \ge 3$ coincide
  here), and let $G$ be a loopless multigraph each of whose edge
  labels links its selector pair. Then at every normal assignment
  $q$ generic for row independence, the induced framework on $G$ has
  every supporting extensor nonzero.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:moment-curve-general-position, def:panel-support-extensor}
  Fix an edge and its two (distinct, since $G$ is loopless)
  endpoints. At the seed assignment placing one endpoint at the
  moment-curve point with parameter $0$ and every other body
  (in particular the other endpoint) at parameter $1$, the two
  endpoint normals are linearly independent
  (\cref{lem:moment-curve-general-position}), so the edge's
  supporting extensor is nonzero at the seed
  (\cref{def:panel-support-extensor}). Since $D \ge 2$, some
  exterior-basis coordinate of a nonzero extensor is nonzero, and
  the annihilator row pairing that coordinate with any second one
  reads it off directly, so it is a nonzero row at the seed; a
  one-row subfamily nonzero at the seed is linearly independent
  there, hence --- by genericity --- linearly independent, i.e.\
  nonzero, at $q$. The row is linear in the supporting extensor, so
  a nonzero row at $q$ forces the extensor at $q$ to be nonzero.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[A witness realization transplants into the normal
  coordinates]
  \label{lem:panel-witness-transplant}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.exists_independent_normalRow_of_le_finrank}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:panel-hinge-framework, def:rigidity-matrix,
    def:panel-support-extensor}
  Let $G$ be a multigraph each of whose edge labels links its
  selector pair, and let $(G,p)$ be a panel-hinge realization of $G$
  with every supporting extensor nonzero whose own endpoint selector
  also records a link of every edge. Then for every
  $N \le \operatorname{rank} R(G,p)$ there are $N$ indices of the
  annihilator-row family that are linearly independent at the normal
  assignment reading off $(G,p)$'s panel normals.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}
  Under nonzero extensors the annihilator rows over the fixed
  selector span the row space of $R(G,p)$, so an independent
  subfamily of $N$ actual rows of $(G,p)$ can be extracted and
  re-indexed by edge and basis pair, as in
  \cref{lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract} (in the rank-input
  form discussed after that lemma, consuming the rank bound rather
  than rigidity). At each edge the realization's selector and the
  fixed selector record the same unordered pair of endpoints, and
  swapping the two panels negates their meet
  (\cref{def:panel-support-extensor}), so each extracted row agrees
  up to sign with the corresponding member of the annihilator-row
  family at the read-off assignment; linear independence is
  unchanged by signs.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[The deficiency rank at every generic normal
  assignment]
  \label{thm:panel-generic-rank}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.finrank_span_rigidityRows_ofNormals_of_isGenericNormals}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-normals, def:panel-hinge-framework,
    def:rank-hypothesis, def:D-deficiency}
  Let $G$ be a simple spanning multigraph on at least two bodies, at
  any dimension $n \ge 3$ (equivalently $D = \binom{n+1}{2} \ge 6$)
  and over any infinite field $K$, with a link-recording endpoint
  selector. Then every normal assignment $q$ generic for row
  independence attains the deficiency rank:
  \[
    \operatorname{rank} R(G,q)
      \;=\; D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G).
  \]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine, lem:panel-witness-transplant,
    lem:generic-normals-nondegenerate, prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}
  For the lower bound: the link-recording nondegenerate witness of
  \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine} attains
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G,p) = D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde
  G)$, so by \cref{lem:panel-witness-transplant} that many indices
  of the annihilator-row family are linearly independent at one
  normal assignment, hence at $q$ (\cref{def:generic-normals}), and
  the annihilator rows of a link-recording selector are rows of
  $R(G,q)$. The upper bound is the genericity-free bound
  $\dim Z(G,q) \ge D + \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ of
  \cref{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}, whose nondegenerate-hinge
  hypothesis holds at $q$ by
  \cref{lem:generic-normals-nondegenerate}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Every generic panel-and-hinge realization of a
  rigid graph; Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{cor:panel-generic-rigid}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.PanelHingeFramework.isInfinitesimallyRigidOn_ofNormals_isGenericNormals_iff}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-normals, def:panel-hinge-framework,
    def:D-deficiency}
  In the setting of \cref{thm:panel-generic-rank}, the framework at
  every normal assignment generic for row independence is
  infinitesimally rigid on $V(G)$ if and only if
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:panel-generic-rank, lem:exists-generic-normals,
    lem:isInfRigidOn-of-relative-count,
    lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}
  If $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$, then at a generic $q$
  \cref{thm:panel-generic-rank} gives
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G,q) = D(|V|-1)$, so
  $\dim Z(G,q) = D|V| - D(|V|-1) = D$ (the graph is spanning) and
  the framework is rigid on $V(G)$
  (\cref{lem:isInfRigidOn-of-relative-count}). Conversely a generic
  assignment exists over the infinite field
  (\cref{lem:exists-generic-normals}); rigidity on $V(G)$ there
  forces $\operatorname{rank} R(G,q) = D(|V|-1)$ (the rank--nullity
  count of \cref{lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}), which
  compared with \cref{thm:panel-generic-rank} gives
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$.
\end{proof}

This is the sharp form of Jackson and Jord\'an's
Theorem~7.2~\cite[\S7]{jacksonJordan2009}: their sufficient
condition --- $d$ edge-disjoint spanning trees in $(d-1)G$ ---
implies $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$, by repeating one further
spanning tree of $G$ over the remaining multiplicities to produce
$D$ edge-disjoint spanning trees in $(D-1)G$, as in their
Theorem~6.5. The tree-packing reformulation of the deficiency-zero
condition is taken up with the body-and-hinge form of the lift.

\subsection{Every generic body-and-bar realization}
\label{sec:generic-lift-bodybar}

A body-and-bar realization of a multigraph places each bar $e$ as a
line segment between two endpoints $p_e, p'_e \in \R^n$, and the
constraint row of $e$ pairs the two-extensor of that segment
against the relative velocity of the two bodies it
joins~\cite[\S5]{jacksonJordan2009}. The body-bar chapter works
with a more permissive model: a framework of
\cref{def:body-bar-framework} carries an arbitrary vector of
$\R^d$, $d = n(n+1)/2$, on each bar. The realization parameters of
this section are instead the $2n$ endpoint coordinates of each bar,
so that ``almost all'' quantifies over bar endpoint choices, as in
Jackson and Jord\'an. The two-extensor of a segment is a polynomial
map of its endpoints (\cref{def:two-extensor}), so the rigidity
rows are polynomial in the endpoint coordinates and the abundance
argument of \cref{lem:generic-placement-abundance} applies
unchanged.

Not every framework of \cref{def:body-bar-framework} arises from
endpoints: a two-extensor whose direction block vanishes but whose
moment block does not --- a line at infinity --- is the two-extensor
of no segment, since a segment with zero direction has equal
endpoints and zero moment as well. In particular the standard-basis
witness frameworks of \cref{prop:tay-witness-exists} are not
endpoint realizations, and the existence theorems do not lift into
the endpoint parameter space by specialization alone. The witness
inside the endpoint space is Jackson and Jord\'an's Lemma~5.1: bars
placed on segments between the origin and the standard basis points
of $\R^n$. We obtain it from the standard-basis witness by a change
of coordinates on the extensor space
(\cref{lem:coordinate-extensor-basis} and
\cref{lem:extensor-map-rows}), the route Jackson and Jord\'an
attribute to Whiteley: the coordinate-segment two-extensors are the
image of the standard basis of $\R^d$ under a single invertible
linear map, and applying a fixed invertible map to the extensor
coordinate of every bar preserves independence of the
rows~\cite[\S5, Remark]{jacksonJordan2009}.

\begin{definition}[The two-extensor of a segment]
  \label{def:two-extensor}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.pairIdxEquiv, Graph.BodyBarFramework.twoExtensor,
    Graph.BodyBarFramework.twoExtensorPoly}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:body-bar-framework}
  For $p \in \R^n$ let $h(p) = (1, p) \in \R^{n+1}$ be its
  homogeneous lift, with coordinates indexed by $0, 1, \dots, n$.
  Fix an enumeration of the pairs $0 \le i < j \le n$ by the
  $d = n(n+1)/2$ coordinates of the extensor space $\R^d$ of
  \cref{def:body-bar-framework}. The \emph{two-extensor} of an
  ordered pair of points $p, p' \in \R^n$ is the vector
  $T(p, p') \in \R^d$ of $2 \times 2$ minors
  \[
    T(p, p')_{(i,j)} \;=\; h(p)_i\, h(p')_j - h(p)_j\, h(p')_i,
    \qquad 0 \le i < j \le n.
  \]
  The \emph{direction} coordinates $(0, j)$ equal $p'_j - p_j$,
  polynomials of degree one in the $2n$ endpoint coordinates; the
  \emph{moment} coordinates $(i, j)$ with $i \ge 1$ equal
  $p_i p'_j - p_j p'_i$, of degree two.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Endpoint realization; generic endpoint
  assignment]
  \label{def:generic-endpoints}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.ofEndpoints, Graph.BodyBarFramework.IsGenericEndpoints}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:two-extensor, def:body-bar-framework,
    def:rigidity-map-body-bar}
  Fix a multigraph $G$ and an orientation of $G$
  (\cref{def:rigidity-map-body-bar}; the orientation fixes row
  signs only). An \emph{endpoint assignment} $q$ gives each bar an
  ordered pair of points of $\R^n$; its \emph{endpoint realization}
  is the body-bar framework placing each bar $e \in E(G)$ at the
  two-extensor of its assigned pair. The assignment $q$ is
  \emph{generic for row independence} if every set of bars whose
  rigidity rows are linearly independent at the endpoint
  realization of some assignment has linearly independent rows at
  the endpoint realization of $q$.
\end{definition}

As in the preceding sections, a generic assignment attains the
maximum, over all endpoint assignments, of the rank of the rigidity
matrix and of every edge-induced submatrix, which is Jackson and
Jord\'an's definition of a generic body-and-bar
realization~\cite[\S5]{jacksonJordan2009}. The transfer runs over
endpoint assignments only: independence witnessed by a framework
outside the endpoint parameter space does not transfer, which is
why the witness lemma below re-places the standard-basis witness
inside that space.

\begin{lemma}[Abundance of generic endpoint assignments]
  \label{lem:generic-endpoints-abundance}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.exists_isGenericEndpoints_abundance}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-endpoints}
  There is a nonzero polynomial $P$ in the $2n\,|E(G)|$ endpoint
  coordinates such that every endpoint assignment $q$ with
  $P(q) \ne 0$ is generic for row independence. In particular the
  non-generic assignments are contained in the zero set of a
  nonzero polynomial.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:two-extensor}
  Each coordinate of the rigidity row of a bar at an endpoint
  realization is, up to an incidence sign, a coordinate of the
  two-extensor of its assigned pair, hence a polynomial of degree
  at most two in the endpoint coordinates (\cref{def:two-extensor}).
  The proof of \cref{lem:generic-placement-abundance} applies
  verbatim: for each subfamily of bars whose rows are independent
  at some assignment, a maximal square minor of the row-coordinate
  matrix, chosen nonsingular there, is a nonzero polynomial whose
  non-vanishing at an assignment suffices for independence, and $P$
  is the product of these witnessing minors over the finitely many
  such subfamilies.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Existence of generic endpoint assignments]
  \label{lem:exists-generic-endpoints}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.exists_isGenericEndpoints}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-endpoints}
  Generic endpoint assignments exist.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:generic-endpoints-abundance}
  A nonzero real polynomial has a non-root, and every non-root of
  the polynomial of \cref{lem:generic-endpoints-abundance} is
  generic.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[The coordinate-segment two-extensors form a basis]
  \label{lem:coordinate-extensor-basis}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.coordPoint,
    Graph.BodyBarFramework.linearIndependent_twoExtensor_coordPoint}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:two-extensor}
  Let $c_0 = 0 \in \R^n$ and let $c_i$ be the $i$-th standard basis
  point of $\R^n$ for $1 \le i \le n$. The $d$ two-extensors
  $T(c_i, c_j)$, $0 \le i < j \le n$, are linearly independent, and
  hence form a basis of $\R^d$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  From the minor formula, $T(c_0, c_k)$ is the coordinate vector
  supported at the direction coordinate $(0, k)$, while for
  $1 \le h < k \le n$ the two-extensor $T(c_h, c_k)$ has exactly
  three nonzero coordinates: $+1$ at the moment coordinate
  $(h, k)$, $+1$ at $(0, k)$ and $-1$ at $(0, h)$ --- the entry
  table in the proof of Lemma~5.1
  of~\cite[\S5]{jacksonJordan2009}. Every standard basis vector of
  $\R^d$ is therefore a combination of the two-extensors: the
  direction-coordinate vector at $(0, k)$ is $T(c_0, c_k)$ itself,
  and the moment-coordinate vector at $(h, k)$ is
  $T(c_h, c_k)$ plus the already-available direction vector at
  $(0, h)$ minus the one at $(0, k)$. So the two-extensors span
  $\R^d$; a spanning family of $d$ vectors in the
  $d$-dimensional $\R^d$ is a basis, hence linearly independent.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Row independence under a change of extensor
  coordinates]
  \label{lem:extensor-map-rows}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.mapPlacement,
    Graph.BodyBarFramework.linearIndependent_rigidityRow_mapPlacement}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:body-bar-framework, def:rigidity-map-body-bar}
  Let $F$ be a body-bar framework and $M$ an invertible linear map
  of the extensor space $\R^d$. Applying $M$ to the extensor
  coordinate of every bar preserves linear independence of every
  subfamily of rigidity rows.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  The row of a bar $e$ at the transformed framework evaluates a
  motion $m$ to $\langle M b_e,\, m_u - m_v\rangle
  = \langle b_e,\, M^{*} m_u - M^{*} m_v\rangle$, where $M^{*}$ is
  the adjoint of $M$: the transformed row is the original row
  precomposed with the invertible change of motion coordinates
  applying $M^{*}$ bodywise. Precomposition with a fixed invertible
  map is an injective linear map of the dual of the motion space,
  and so preserves linear independence.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Coordinate-segment witness; Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{lem:endpoint-witness}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.exists_endpoints_linearIndependent_rigidityRow}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-endpoints, def:graph-sparse}
  Let $E' \subseteq E(G)$ be a bar set whose edge-restriction
  $G \restriction E'$ is $(d, d)$-sparse. Then there is an endpoint
  assignment whose rigidity rows indexed by $E'$ are linearly
  independent.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:k-frame-forest-packing-of-sparse,
    lem:coordinate-extensor-basis, lem:extensor-map-rows,
    prop:tay-witness-exists}
  If $E' = \emptyset$ the claim is vacuous; assume $E'$ nonempty.
  Sparsity decomposes $E'$ into $d$ disjoint forests
  (\cref{lem:k-frame-forest-packing-of-sparse}, disjointified),
  re-indexed by the pairs $0 \le i < j \le n$; assign each bar of
  the $(i, j)$-th forest the segment from $c_i$ to $c_j$, and
  extend the forest-index map arbitrarily off $E'$. The resulting
  endpoint realization places bar $e$ at $T(c_{i(e)}, c_{j(e)})$,
  the image of the standard-basis placement at the pair-valued
  forest-index map under the change of extensor coordinates
  carrying the standard basis of $\R^d$ to the coordinate-segment
  two-extensors (\cref{lem:coordinate-extensor-basis}). The
  standard-basis rows indexed by $E'$ are linearly independent by
  the block-diagonal count in the proof of
  \cref{prop:tay-witness-exists}, restricted to the subfamily
  indexed by $E'$: each row is supported in the coordinate block of
  its forest, where it is the signed incidence row of its bar, and
  a forest's incidence rows are independent.
  \Cref{lem:extensor-map-rows} transports this independence to the
  coordinate-segment rows. (Jackson and Jord\'an prove the lemma by
  a staged elimination on the entries of the $T(c_i, c_j)$; the
  change-of-coordinates route is the one they attribute to
  Whiteley~\cite[\S5, Remark]{jacksonJordan2009}.)
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Independence at a generic endpoint assignment]
  \label{thm:bodybar-generic-independence}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.linearIndependent_rigidityRow_ofEndpoints_iff}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-endpoints, def:graph-sparse}
  Let $q$ be an endpoint assignment generic for row independence.
  Then for every bar set $E' \subseteq E(G)$, the rigidity rows of
  the endpoint realization of $q$ indexed by $E'$ are linearly
  independent if and only if the edge-restriction
  $G \restriction E'$ is $(d, d)$-sparse.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:endpoint-witness, lem:tay-isSparse-of-independent}
  If $G \restriction E'$ is sparse, some endpoint assignment has
  independent rows on $E'$ (\cref{lem:endpoint-witness}), so the
  generic $q$ does. Conversely, independent rows on $E'$ force
  sparsity of $G \restriction E'$ with no genericity: as in the
  proof of \cref{lem:tay-isSparse-of-independent}, restricted to
  $E'$, every non-empty $E'' \subseteq E'$ has its $|E''|$
  independent rows inside the block-diagonal product subspace of
  dimension $d \cdot r(E'')$, and $r(E'') + 1 \le |V''|$ gives the
  sparsity count $|E''| + d \le d\,|V''|$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Tay's theorem at every generic endpoint
  assignment; Tay, Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{cor:bodybar-generic-tay}
  \lean{Graph.BodyBarFramework.isIndependent_ofEndpoints_iff,
    Graph.BodyBarFramework.isIndependent_and_isInfinitesimallyRigid_ofEndpoints_iff}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-endpoints, def:independent-body-bar,
    def:infinitesimally-rigid-body-bar, def:graph-sparse}
  Let $q$ be an endpoint assignment generic for row independence.
  The endpoint realization of $q$ is independent if and only if $G$
  is $(d, d)$-sparse, and it is isostatic --- independent and
  infinitesimally rigid --- if and only if $G$ is $(d, d)$-tight.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:bodybar-generic-independence,
    lem:tay-isSparse-of-independent}
  Take $E' = E(G)$ in \cref{thm:bodybar-generic-independence}:
  sparsity of $G$ makes all $|E(G)|$ rows independent, and
  $|E(G)|$ independent rows are full row rank, i.e.\ independence;
  the converse is \cref{lem:tay-isSparse-of-independent} directly,
  with no genericity. For the isostatic form, independence gives
  $\operatorname{rank} = |E|$, under which the tightness count
  $|E| + d = d\,|V|$ and the rigidity count
  $\operatorname{rank} + d = d\,|V|$ coincide, as in the proof of
  \cref{thm:tay-witness}.
\end{proof}

This upgrades the existence quantifiers of \cref{thm:tay-witness}
to every generic endpoint assignment, the generic form of Tay's
theorem~\cite{tay1984}, proved coordinate-wise as
in~\cite[\S5]{jacksonJordan2009}. Two consequences worth recording
in prose. First, every generic endpoint realization of a multigraph
containing $d$ edge-disjoint spanning trees is infinitesimally
rigid: the union of the trees is a bar set whose edge-restriction
is $(d, d)$-tight, so its $d(|V| - 1)$ rows are independent at $q$
by \cref{thm:bodybar-generic-independence}, while the
block-diagonal bound caps the rank of any body-bar rigidity matrix
at $d(|V| - 1)$, forcing the rigidity count. Second,
\cref{thm:bodybar-generic-independence} identifies the bar sets
with independent rows at a generic assignment with the independent
sets of the $d$-fold union of the cycle matroid
(\cref{thm:unionPow-cycle-indep-iff-sparse}); the rank formula of
Jackson and Jord\'an's Theorem~5.2 (which they attribute to Tay) is
the rank of that union.

\subsection{Every generic body-and-hinge realization}
\label{sec:generic-lift-bodyhinge}

A body-and-hinge realization of a multigraph assigns each edge a
$(d-2)$-dimensional affine hinge subspace of $K^d$, $d = k+1$. In
the affine model of \cref{def:hinge-constraint} the hinge at an edge
is recorded by $k$ points of $K^{k+1}$ spanning it, and the
supporting extensor is the join of their homogenizations
(\cref{def:affine-subspace-extensor}), so the realization parameters
form the $k(k+1)\,|E|$-coordinate space of \emph{hinge-point
assignments}, and ``almost all'' quantifies over hinge-point
choices, following the body-and-hinge section of Jackson and
Jord\'an~\cite[\S6]{jacksonJordan2009}. As in the panel section, a
multigraph's edges carry their endpoints only relationally, so the
row family below is indexed through a fixed endpoint selector.

The screw-space coordinates of the supporting extensor of a hinge
are the Pl\"ucker coordinates of its homogenized spanning points ---
$k \times k$ minors of their coordinate matrix
(\cref{def:plucker-coords}) --- and one column of that matrix is the
homogenizing constants (\cref{def:homogeneous-coords}), so each
coordinate is a polynomial of degree at most $k$ in the hinge-point
coordinates (\cref{lem:hinge-rows-polynomial-in-points}). The
annihilator rows are linear in the supporting extensor, so the
rigidity rows are again polynomial in the realization parameters and
the abundance argument of \cref{lem:generic-placement-abundance}
applies unchanged.

Not every framework of \cref{def:hinge-constraint} arises from hinge
points: a nonzero decomposable supporting extensor whose
$k$-dimensional homogeneous span lies inside the hyperplane at
infinity --- a \emph{hinge at infinity} --- is the join of no
homogenized point family, the body-and-hinge counterpart of the
body-and-bar line at infinity of \cref{sec:generic-lift-bodybar}.
The witness realization of \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine} carries
its hinges as meets of panels, which the construction does not keep
away from the infinite hyperplane, so the witness enters the
parameter space through a change of coordinates: one invertible map
of the homogeneous space moves all the finitely many hinges off
infinity simultaneously (\cref{lem:simultaneous-affine-position}),
affine spanning points are then read off each moved hinge
(\cref{lem:extensor-affine-representation}), and the rank of the
rigidity-row span is unchanged under the induced map of the screw
space (\cref{lem:screw-map-rows}). The simultaneous move parallels
the choice of coordinate system in the proof of Lemma~7.1 of Jackson
and Jord\'an~\cite{jacksonJordan2009}; their own witness inside the
hinge parameter space --- their Theorem~6.4, which places every
hinge on a facet of the coordinate simplex --- is not re-proved
here.

\begin{lemma}[The rigidity rows are polynomial in the hinge points]
  \label{lem:hinge-rows-polynomial-in-points}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.hingeExtensorPoly,
    CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.hingeExtensorPoly_eval}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:affine-subspace-extensor, def:plucker-coords,
    def:homogeneous-coords}
  Fix a finite edge label set. For each edge label $e$ and each
  $k$-element set $t$ of homogeneous coordinate indices there is a
  polynomial of degree at most $k$ in the $k(k+1)\,|E|$ hinge-point
  coordinates whose value at every hinge-point assignment $q$ is the
  $t$-th standard exterior-basis coordinate of the supporting
  extensor of the hinge of $e$, the join of the homogenizations of
  the $k$ points that $q$ assigns to $e$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:extensor}
  The $t$-th exterior-basis coordinate of a join of $k$ vectors of
  $K^{k+2}$ is the $k \times k$ minor of their coordinate matrix at
  the columns of $t$ (\cref{def:plucker-coords}). With the rows the
  homogenized points, each entry of that matrix is either a
  hinge-point coordinate or the homogenizing constant $1$
  (\cref{def:homogeneous-coords}), a polynomial of degree at most
  one, so the minor is a polynomial of degree at most $k$; the
  evaluation identity holds because evaluation commutes with the
  determinant.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Generic hinge-point assignment]
  \label{def:generic-hinge-points}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.hingePointRow,
    CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.hingePointRow_eq_panelRow,
    CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.IsGenericHingePoints}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:hinge-constraint, def:rigidity-matrix,
    def:affine-subspace-extensor}
  Fix finite body and edge label sets and an endpoint selector. For
  a hinge-point assignment $q$, the \emph{annihilator-row family} of
  $q$ is the family of rigidity rows of the body-and-hinge framework
  whose hinge at each edge is spanned by the points $q$ assigns to
  it (\cref{def:hinge-constraint}), indexed by an edge label
  together with a pair of grade-$k$ exterior basis indices, exactly
  as in \cref{def:generic-normals}. The assignment $q$ is
  \emph{generic for row independence} if every subfamily of the
  annihilator-row family that is linearly independent at some
  hinge-point assignment is linearly independent at $q$.
\end{definition}

As in the panel case, the annihilator-row family reads only the
endpoint selector and the assignment, so genericity is a property of
the assignment alone, and the transfer runs over hinge-point
assignments only: independence witnessed by a framework outside the
hinge-point parameter space --- in particular by one with a hinge at
infinity --- does not transfer, which is why the witness lemma below
re-places the panel witness inside that space.

\begin{lemma}[Abundance of generic hinge-point assignments]
  \label{lem:generic-hinge-points-abundance}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.exists_isGenericHingePoints_abundance}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-hinge-points, lem:affine-indep-iff}
  There is a nonzero polynomial $P$ in the $k(k+1)\,|E|$ hinge-point
  coordinates such that every hinge-point assignment $q$ with
  $P(q) \ne 0$ is generic for row independence \emph{and} assigns
  each edge $k$ affinely independent points --- a genuine hinge. In
  particular the assignments that are non-generic, or degenerate at
  some hinge, are contained in the zero set of one nonzero
  polynomial.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:hinge-rows-polynomial-in-points,
    def:affine-subspace-extensor}
  The row coordinates are polynomials of degree at most $k$ in the
  hinge-point coordinates
  (\cref{lem:hinge-rows-polynomial-in-points}), so for each
  subfamily of row indices independent at some assignment, a maximal
  square minor of the row-coordinate matrix, chosen nonsingular
  there, is a nonzero polynomial whose non-vanishing at an
  assignment suffices for independence --- the witnessing minor of
  \cref{lem:generic-placement-abundance}'s proof. The genuine-hinge
  conjunct contributes one further factor per edge: at the reference
  assignment placing every hinge at a fixed affinely independent
  $k$-point family of $K^{k+1}$, the supporting extensor is nonzero
  (\cref{lem:affine-indep-iff} through
  \cref{def:affine-subspace-extensor}), so some exterior coordinate
  --- one of the minor polynomials of
  \cref{lem:hinge-rows-polynomial-in-points} --- is nonzero at the
  reference, and its non-vanishing at $q$ makes the extensor of that
  edge nonzero, i.e.\ the assigned points affinely independent. $P$
  is the product of the witnessing minors over the finitely many
  independent subfamilies together with one such reference minor per
  edge; a finite product of nonzero polynomials is nonzero.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Existence of generic hinge-point assignments]
  \label{lem:exists-generic-hinge-points}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.exists_isGenericHingePoints}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-hinge-points}
  Over an infinite field, there is a hinge-point assignment that is
  generic for row independence and assigns each edge $k$ affinely
  independent points.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:generic-hinge-points-abundance}
  A nonzero polynomial over an infinite field has a non-root, and
  every non-root of the polynomial of
  \cref{lem:generic-hinge-points-abundance} is such an assignment.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Generic hinge-point assignments have nondegenerate
  hinges]
  \label{lem:generic-hinge-points-nondegenerate}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.supportExtensor_ofHinge_ne_zero_of_isGenericHingePoints}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-hinge-points, def:hinge-constraint}
  Let $k \ge 1$ (so $D \ge 2$), and let $G$ be a loopless multigraph
  each of whose edge labels links its selector pair. Then at every
  hinge-point assignment $q$ generic for row independence, the
  induced framework on $G$ has every supporting extensor nonzero.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{lem:affine-indep-iff, def:affine-subspace-extensor}
  Fix an edge. At the reference assignment placing every hinge at a
  fixed affinely independent $k$-point family, the edge's supporting
  extensor is nonzero (\cref{lem:affine-indep-iff}); since
  $D \ge 2$, some exterior-basis coordinate of a nonzero extensor is
  nonzero, and the annihilator row pairing that coordinate with any
  second one reads it off directly, so it is a nonzero row at the
  reference --- nonzero as a functional on motion assignments
  because the edge's two selector endpoints are distinct ($G$ is
  loopless). A one-row subfamily nonzero at the reference is
  linearly independent there, hence --- by genericity --- nonzero at
  $q$, and the row is linear in the supporting extensor, so the
  extensor at $q$ is nonzero. (In contrast with
  \cref{lem:generic-normals-nondegenerate}, no moment-curve seed is
  needed: the reference points are free parameters.)
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Row rank under a change of screw coordinates]
  \label{lem:screw-map-rows}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.finrank_span_rigidityRows_mapSupport}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:hinge-constraint, def:rigidity-matrix}
  Let $F$ be a body-and-hinge framework and $M$ an invertible linear
  map of the screw space. Replacing the supporting extensor of every
  edge by its image under $M$ leaves the dimension of the span of
  the rigidity rows unchanged.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  The row block of an edge is the annihilator of the line spanned by
  its supporting extensor (\cref{def:rigidity-matrix}); a functional
  annihilates $M C$ exactly when its precomposition with $M$
  annihilates $C$, so each rigidity row of the transformed framework
  is a rigidity row of the original framework precomposed with the
  body-wise application of $M^{-1}$. Precomposition with a fixed
  invertible change of motion coordinates is an invertible linear
  map of the dual of the motion space, so the two row sets span
  subspaces of the same dimension. (Unlike the body-and-bar case,
  \cref{lem:extensor-map-rows}, the indexed annihilator-row family
  is not carried member to member --- its members are pinned to
  exterior-basis coordinate pairs --- but the edge row blocks, and
  hence the row span, are.)
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[An affine hinge spans every extensor off infinity]
  \label{lem:extensor-affine-representation}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.exists_affineSubspaceExtensor_eq_smul_extensor}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:affine-subspace-extensor, def:extensor,
    def:homogeneous-coords}
  Let $u_1, \dots, u_k \in K^{k+2}$ and suppose some $u_i$ has
  nonzero last coordinate. Then there are points
  $p_1, \dots, p_k \in K^{k+1}$ and a nonzero scalar $c$ with
  \[
    C(p_1, \dots, p_k) \;=\; c \cdot (u_1 \vee \dots \vee u_k).
  \]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  After a transposition of the family (a sign, absorbed into $c$)
  assume the last coordinate $\lambda$ of $u_1$ is nonzero. Scale
  $u_1$ by $\lambda^{-1}$; subtract from each other $u_i$ the
  multiple of the scaled $u_1$ matching its last coordinate; then
  add the scaled $u_1$ to each of them. The resulting vectors all
  have last coordinate $1$, i.e.\ are the homogenizations of $k$
  points of $K^{k+1}$ (\cref{def:homogeneous-coords}), and every
  step is a column operation --- scaling one argument, or adding a
  multiple of one argument to another --- under which the join
  changes exactly by the factor $\lambda^{-1}$
  (\cref{def:extensor}).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[A simultaneous move off the hyperplane at infinity]
  \label{lem:simultaneous-affine-position}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.exists_linearEquiv_forall_last_ne_zero}
  \leanok
  Let $K$ be infinite and let $w_1, \dots, w_m \in K^{k+2}$ be
  finitely many nonzero vectors. There is an invertible linear map
  $g$ of $K^{k+2}$ such that every $g(w_i)$ has nonzero last
  coordinate.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  The product over $i$ of the linear forms
  $x \mapsto \langle w_i, x \rangle$ is a nonzero polynomial in the
  coordinates of $x$ (each factor is a nonzero form since
  $w_i \ne 0$), so over the infinite $K$ it has a non-root $n_0$:
  a vector with $\langle w_i, n_0 \rangle \ne 0$ for every $i$. Any
  invertible $g$ whose last coordinate functional is
  $x \mapsto \langle x, n_0 \rangle$ --- extend a basis of the
  kernel of that functional by one vector on which it takes the
  value $1$ --- has the required property.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[A hinge-point witness attains the deficiency rank]
  \label{lem:hinge-point-witness}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.exists_hingePoints_independent_hingePointRow}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-hinge-points, def:D-deficiency}
  Let $G$ be a simple spanning multigraph on at least two bodies, at
  any dimension $n \ge 3$ and over any infinite field $K$, with a
  link-recording endpoint selector. Then there is a hinge-point
  assignment at which some
  $D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ indices of the
  annihilator-row family are linearly independent.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine, def:panel-support-extensor,
    def:genuine-hinge-realization, lem:simultaneous-affine-position,
    lem:extensor-affine-representation, lem:screw-map-rows,
    lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}
  The link-recording nondegenerate witness of
  \cref{thm:theorem-55-6-genuine} attains
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G,p) = D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde
  G)$, and each of its supporting extensors, the meet of two
  transversal panels, is the join of $k$ points of the homogeneous
  space (the meet decomposition of
  \cref{def:genuine-hinge-realization} at the panels of
  \cref{def:panel-support-extensor}). Choose a change of coordinates
  moving the leading spanning point of every hinge off the
  hyperplane at infinity (\cref{lem:simultaneous-affine-position};
  the points are nonzero, being members of linearly independent
  families); the induced invertible map of the screw space carries
  each supporting extensor to the join of its moved points and
  leaves the rank of the rigidity-row span unchanged
  (\cref{lem:screw-map-rows}). Every moved hinge is spanned, up to a
  nonzero scalar, by the homogenizations of $k$ affine points
  (\cref{lem:extensor-affine-representation}); the hinge constraint
  reads only the span of the supporting extensor, so the hinge-point
  framework on those points has the same edge row blocks and hence a
  rigidity-row span of the same rank. Extracting that many
  independent annihilator rows over the fixed link-recording
  selector, as in \cref{lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract} (in
  the rank-input form used by \cref{lem:panel-witness-transplant}),
  gives the independent subfamily at the constructed assignment.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[The deficiency rank at every generic hinge-point
  assignment]
  \label{thm:bodyhinge-generic-rank}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.finrank_span_rigidityRows_ofHinge_of_isGenericHingePoints}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-hinge-points, def:hinge-constraint,
    def:D-deficiency}
  Let $G$ be a simple spanning multigraph on at least two bodies, at
  any dimension $n \ge 3$ (equivalently $D = \binom{n+1}{2} \ge 6$)
  and over any infinite field $K$, with a link-recording endpoint
  selector. Then every hinge-point assignment $q$ generic for row
  independence attains the deficiency rank:
  \[
    \operatorname{rank} R(G,q)
      \;=\; D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G).
  \]
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{lem:hinge-point-witness,
    lem:generic-hinge-points-nondegenerate,
    prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}
  For the lower bound: the witness assignment of
  \cref{lem:hinge-point-witness} has
  $D(|V|-1) - \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ linearly independent
  indices of the annihilator-row family, so the generic $q$ does
  (\cref{def:generic-hinge-points}), and the annihilator rows of a
  link-recording selector are rows of $R(G,q)$. The upper bound is
  the genericity-free bound
  $\dim Z(G,q) \ge D + \operatorname{def}(\tilde G)$ of
  \cref{prop:rigidity-matrix-prop11}, whose nondegenerate-hinge
  hypothesis holds at $q$ by
  \cref{lem:generic-hinge-points-nondegenerate}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Every generic body-and-hinge realization of a
  rigid graph; Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{cor:bodyhinge-generic-rigid}
  \lean{CombinatorialRigidity.Molecular.BodyHingeFramework.isInfinitesimallyRigidOn_ofHinge_isGenericHingePoints_iff}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:generic-hinge-points, def:hinge-constraint,
    def:D-deficiency}
  In the setting of \cref{thm:bodyhinge-generic-rank}, the framework
  at every hinge-point assignment generic for row independence is
  infinitesimally rigid on $V(G)$ if and only if
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{thm:bodyhinge-generic-rank, lem:exists-generic-hinge-points,
    lem:isInfRigidOn-of-relative-count,
    lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}
  If $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$, then at a generic $q$
  \cref{thm:bodyhinge-generic-rank} gives
  $\operatorname{rank} R(G,q) = D(|V|-1)$, so
  $\dim Z(G,q) = D|V| - D(|V|-1) = D$ (the graph is spanning) and
  the framework is rigid on $V(G)$
  (\cref{lem:isInfRigidOn-of-relative-count}). Conversely a generic
  assignment exists over the infinite field
  (\cref{lem:exists-generic-hinge-points}); rigidity on $V(G)$ there
  forces $\operatorname{rank} R(G,q) = D(|V|-1)$ (the rank--nullity
  count of \cref{lem:case-II-placement-old-rows-extract}), which
  compared with \cref{thm:bodyhinge-generic-rank} gives
  $\operatorname{def}(\tilde G) = 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Generic body-and-hinge rigidity from spanning
  trees; Tay, Whiteley, Jackson--Jord\'an]
  \label{cor:bodyhinge-generic-tree-packing}
  \uses{def:generic-hinge-points, def:matroid-MG}
  In the setting of \cref{thm:bodyhinge-generic-rank}, the framework
  at every hinge-point assignment generic for row independence is
  infinitesimally rigid on $V(G)$ if and only if
  $\tilde G = (D-1) \cdot G$ contains $D$ pairwise edge-disjoint
  spanning trees.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{cor:bodyhinge-generic-rigid,
    lem:deficiency-zero-iff-tree-packing}
  Combine \cref{cor:bodyhinge-generic-rigid} with the tree-packing
  reformulation of the deficiency-zero condition
  (\cref{lem:deficiency-zero-iff-tree-packing}).
\end{proof}

This is the generic form of the body-and-hinge realization theorem:
the existence form --- a graph has some infinitesimally rigid
body-and-hinge realization if and only if $(D-1) \cdot G$ contains
$D$ edge-disjoint spanning trees --- is due to Tay~\cite{tay1989}
and Whiteley~\cite{whiteley1988}, is recorded as Corollary~6.3
of~\cite{jacksonJordan2009}, and appears in this development as the
bar-bundle reduction statement \cref{thm:body-hinge-tay}. The
present corollary is the geometric statement on the affine-hinge
frameworks of \cref{def:hinge-constraint}, with the existential
quantifier upgraded to every generic hinge-point realization,
following Jackson and Jord\'an's Theorem~6.1 and their notion of a
generic body-and-hinge realization~\cite[\S6]{jacksonJordan2009}.
