\section{The planar rigidity matroid}
\label{sec:rigidity-matroid}

\Cref{sec:laman-theorem} proved the ``easy direction'' of
Lov\'asz--Yemini~\cite{lovaszYemini1982}: in dimension $2$, a
row-independent edge set is automatically $(2, 3)$-sparse
(\cref{lem:isSparse-of-rowIndependent-two}). This chapter completes
the matroid identification by proving the harder converse --- every
$(2, 3)$-sparse edge set is row-independent at some placement --- and
packages the resulting independence structure as a \texttt{Matroid}
instance on the off-diagonal symmetric pairs over $V$.

The proof follows the classical strategy of Lov\'asz--Yemini, in the
modern presentation of Jord\'an~\cite{jordan2016}~\S 2.2 (Theorem
2.2.1): induction on $|E|$, with each Henneberg move on a sparse graph
lifting row-independence to a fresh placement of the larger graph by
placing the new vertex generically. The combinatorial reverse step
(Jord\'an Lemma 2.1.4) --- a sparse graph on $|V| \ge 1$ with $|E| \ge 1$
admits either a Type~I reverse (delete a degree-${\le}2$ vertex) or a
Type~II reverse (split a degree-$3$ vertex) yielding a smaller sparse
graph --- is the sparse-graph analogue of Phase~5 milestone~1
(\cref{thm:isLaman-exists-typeI-or-typeII-reverse}) and reuses the same
tight-subset machinery (\cref{thm:isTightOn-union-inter}).

\emph{Terminology.} Throughout this chapter we use the project's
\emph{$(2, 3)$-tight subset} (\cref{def:isTightOn}) in place of
Jord\'an's \emph{critical set}. The two are interchangeable: a
critical set in a $(2, 3)$-sparse graph $G$ is exactly a subset
$X \subseteq V$ with $\bigl|\edgesIn{X}\bigr| = 2|X| - 3$, i.e.\
\texttt{G.IsTightOn 2 3 X}. Likewise Jord\'an's Lemma~2.1.2
(critical sets are union/intersection-closed when intersections have
$\ge 2$ vertices) is our \cref{thm:isTightOn-union-inter}; the
three-pair tight-union (Jord\'an Lemma~2.1.3) is
\cref{thm:isTightOn-union-with-bonus} composed twice.

\subsection{Sparse-graph reverse decomposition}
\label{sec:rigidity-matroid-sparse-reverse}

\begin{theorem}[Sparse-graph reverse decomposition]
  \label{thm:isSparse-exists-typeI-or-typeII-reverse}
  \lean{SimpleGraph.IsSparse.exists_typeI_or_typeII_reverse}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:isSparse, def:typeI, def:typeII}
  Let $V$ be finite and $G : \mathrm{SimpleGraph}\,V$ be $(2, 3)$-sparse
  with at least one edge. Then either
  \begin{enumerate}
    \item there exists $v \in V$ with $d_{G}(v) \le 2$ such that
      $G - v$ is $(2, 3)$-sparse (Type~I reverse), or
    \item there exists $v \in V$ with $d_{G}(v) = 3$, neighbours
      $\{u, w, z\} = N_{G}(v)$, and a non-adjacent pair (say
      $\{u, w\}$) such that the graph $G^{u,w}_{v}$ obtained by
      deleting $v$ and adding edge $\{u, w\}$ is $(2, 3)$-sparse
      (Type~II reverse).
  \end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \leanok
  \uses{def:isSparse, def:typeI, def:typeII, thm:isTightOn-union-inter}
  Since $G$ is sparse with $|E| \ge 1$ we have $|E| \le 2|V| - 3$, so
  the average degree is $2|E|/|V| < 4$, hence some vertex has degree
  $\le 3$. (When $|V| < 2$, sparsity is vacuous and $|E| = 0$, so the
  hypothesis $|E| \ge 1$ forces $|V| \ge 2$.) Pick such a $v$. The
  case $d_{G}(v) \le 2$ gives the Type~I reverse: deleting $v$ removes
  at most $2$ edges, and sparsity is preserved (sparse minus edges is
  sparse). The case $d_{G}(v) = 3$ requires a suitable splitting, the
  sparse-graph analogue of Jord\'an Lemma 2.1.4(b): if no splitting at
  $v$ is suitable then there exist three maximal $(2, 3)$-tight sets
  $X_{uw}, X_{uz}, X_{wz} \subseteq V \setminus \{v\}$ each containing
  exactly two neighbours of $v$, with pairwise singleton intersections;
  Phase~1's tight-union closure (\cref{thm:isTightOn-union-inter}) and
  the three-pair tight-union analogue (Jord\'an Lemma 2.1.3) make
  $X_{uw} \cup X_{uz} \cup X_{wz}$ tight, forcing
  $d_{G}(v, X_{uw} \cup X_{uz} \cup X_{wz}) \ge 3$, which then
  contradicts the sparsity bound on
  $X_{uw} \cup X_{uz} \cup X_{wz} \cup \{v\}$.
\end{proof}

\emph{Statement-form aside.} The reverse decomposition above and the
forward lifts below are stated in different forms by design. The
reverse is \emph{flat}: it describes the smaller graph $G'$ by its
edges directly (``$G - v$ is sparse'', ``$G^{u,w}_v$ is sparse''),
without naming a Henneberg constructor. Each forward lift, by
contrast, is in \emph{operation form}: it states a property of the
named extension $\mathrm{typeI}\,G'\,a\,b$ or
$\mathrm{typeII}\,G'\,a\,b\,c$. The asymmetry tracks what each kind
of theorem is \emph{about}. A reverse decomposition takes an
arbitrary $G$ and produces a smaller witness; the named operation is
incidental, so we omit it (mathlib analogue:
\texttt{Finset.exists\_max\_image}). A forward lift is a statement
about the operation itself --- \emph{``$\mathrm{typeI}$ preserves
row-independence''} --- so the operation belongs in the statement
(mathlib analogue: \texttt{Finset.image\_disjoint}). The same split
governs the rest of the project: Phase~5's rigidity-preservation
lemmas (\cref{thm:typeI-isGenericallyRigidInj-two},
\cref{thm:typeII-isGenericallyRigidInj-two}) are operation-form, and
Phase~5's Laman reverse decomposition
(\cref{thm:isLaman-exists-typeI-or-typeII-reverse}) is flat. The two
forms meet only at callsites that combine a reverse decomposition
with a forward lift: the flat reverse produces a
vertex-with-neighborhood witness, and the forward lift is invoked on
the corresponding $\mathrm{typeI}$ / $\mathrm{typeII}$ extension
after a small iso construction (\texttt{typeI\_iso\_of\_two\_neighbors}
or \texttt{typeII\_iso\_of\_three\_neighbors}, both project-internal).
See \texttt{DESIGN.md} \emph{Statement-form conventions} for the
project-wide statement of this principle.

\subsection{Lifting row-independence through Henneberg moves}
\label{sec:rigidity-matroid-lifts}

The two Henneberg lifts are the technical heart of the converse. Each
states: given a placement $p'$ of a smaller graph $G'$ at which the
edge set is row-independent, the larger graph $G$ obtained by a
Henneberg move admits a placement at which its edge set is
row-independent.

\begin{lemma}[Type~I lift of row-independence]
  \label{lem:typeI-rowIndependent-lift}
  \uses{def:typeI, def:edgeSet-rowIndependent}
  Let $V$ be finite, $G'$ a simple graph on $V$, and $p' : V \to \R^{2}$
  a placement at which $E(G')$ is row-independent. Let $G$ be the
  Type~I extension of $G'$ adding a fresh vertex $\star$ adjacent to
  two distinct old vertices $a, b \in V$. Then there exists a
  placement $p$ of $V \sqcup \{\star\}$ with $p|_{V} = p'$ such that
  $E(G)$ is row-independent at $p$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{def:typeI, def:edgeSet-rowIndependent}
  The rigidity matrix of $G$ at $p$ decomposes block-triangularly: the
  rows indexed by the old edges of $G'$ vanish on the $\star$-column
  and reproduce the rigidity matrix of $G'$ at $p'$; the two new rows
  ($\{\star, a\}$ and $\{\star, b\}$) are supported on the $\star$,
  $a$, and $b$ columns. Row-independence of the old block holds by
  hypothesis. Choosing $p(\star)$ so that the two new rows are linearly
  independent on the $\star$-column --- i.e.\ so that
  $p(\star) - p(a)$ and $p(\star) - p(b)$ are linearly independent in
  $\R^{2}$ --- is generic: the bad set is a finite union of one or two
  lines in $\R^{2}$. Block-triangularity then yields row-independence
  at $p$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Type~II lift of row-independence]
  \label{lem:typeII-rowIndependent-lift}
  \uses{def:typeII, def:edgeSet-rowIndependent}
  Let $V$ be finite, $G'$ a simple graph on $V$, $\{u, w\} \in E(G')$,
  and $z \in V$ a third vertex distinct from $u$ and $w$. Let $p' :
  V \to \R^{2}$ be a placement at which $E(G')$ is row-independent.
  Let $G$ be the Type~II extension of $G'$ deleting $\{u, w\}$ and
  adding a fresh vertex $\star$ adjacent to $u, w, z$. Then there
  exists a placement $p$ of $V \sqcup \{\star\}$ with $p|_{V} = p'$
  such that $E(G)$ is row-independent at $p$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{def:typeII, def:edgeSet-rowIndependent,
    lem:isInfinitesimallyRigid-eventually}
  Place $p(\star)$ on the line through $p(u)$ and $p(w)$, generically
  --- specifically away from $p(u), p(w)$, and away from the line
  through $p(z)$ in directions where the third new row would
  collapse. The two new rows $\{\star, u\}$ and $\{\star, w\}$ are
  scalar multiples of the deleted row $\{u, w\}$ (the difference
  vectors $p(\star) - p(u)$ and $p(\star) - p(w)$ are anti-parallel,
  hence each anti-parallel to $p(u) - p(w)$); together they span the
  same one-dimensional row space modulo the $\star$-column structure.
  The third new row $\{\star, z\}$ is independent of the two by
  non-collinearity of $p(u), p(w), p(z)$, which we may arrange by
  perturbing $p'$ in the kernel of the rigidity map (using openness of
  row-independence, the row-independence analogue of
  \cref{lem:isInfinitesimallyRigid-eventually}) if necessary. The net
  effect is to replace the deleted $\{u, w\}$ row by two equivalent
  rows in its span, plus one genuinely new row from $\{\star, z\}$, so
  the rank goes up by exactly $2$ as required by sparsity.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The hard direction}
\label{sec:rigidity-matroid-hard-direction}

\begin{theorem}[$(2, 3)$-sparse $\Rightarrow$ row-independent at some placement]
  \label{thm:isSparse-exists-rowIndependent-placement}
  \uses{def:isSparse, def:edgeSet-rowIndependent,
    thm:isSparse-exists-typeI-or-typeII-reverse,
    lem:typeI-rowIndependent-lift,
    lem:typeII-rowIndependent-lift}
  Let $V$ be finite and let $H : \mathrm{SimpleGraph}\,V$ be
  $(2, 3)$-sparse. Then there exists a placement $p : V \to \R^{2}$ at
  which $E(H)$ is row-independent.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{thm:isSparse-exists-typeI-or-typeII-reverse,
    lem:typeI-rowIndependent-lift,
    lem:typeII-rowIndependent-lift}
  Induction on $|E(H)|$. Base case $|E(H)| = 0$: the empty family of
  rows is linearly independent at any placement. Inductive step
  $|E(H)| \ge 1$: \cref{thm:isSparse-exists-typeI-or-typeII-reverse}
  supplies a smaller sparse graph $H' \le H$ (deletion of a
  degree-${\le}2$ vertex or splitting of a degree-$3$ vertex). The
  inductive hypothesis gives a row-independent placement $p'$ of $H'$;
  \cref{lem:typeI-rowIndependent-lift} or
  \cref{lem:typeII-rowIndependent-lift} (according to the case) lifts
  to a row-independent placement of $H$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Lov\'asz--Yemini: row-LI iff sparse, dim $2$]
  \label{thm:edgeSet-rowIndependent-iff-isSparse}
  \uses{def:edgeSet-rowIndependent, def:isSparse,
    thm:isSparse-exists-rowIndependent-placement,
    lem:isSparse-of-rowIndependent-two,
    lem:exists-affinelySpanning-rigid-placement}
  Let $V$ be finite with $|V| \ge 2$ and $G : \mathrm{SimpleGraph}\,V$.
  An edge set $I \subseteq E(G)$ is row-independent at some placement
  $p : V \to \R^{2}$ if and only if the spanning subgraph of $G$ with
  edge set $I$ is $(2, 3)$-sparse.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{thm:isSparse-exists-rowIndependent-placement,
    lem:isSparse-of-rowIndependent-two,
    lem:exists-affinelySpanning-rigid-placement}
  $(\Rightarrow)$ is the easy direction
  \cref{lem:isSparse-of-rowIndependent-two}, after combining the
  witness placement with the affinely-spanning perturbation of
  \cref{lem:exists-affinelySpanning-rigid-placement} on every triple.
  $(\Leftarrow)$ is \cref{thm:isSparse-exists-rowIndependent-placement}.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Every Laman graph admits a row-independent placement]
  \label{cor:isLaman-exists-rowIndependent}
  \uses{def:isLaman, def:edgeSet-rowIndependent,
    thm:isSparse-exists-rowIndependent-placement}
  Let $V$ be finite with $|V| \ge 2$ and $H : \mathrm{SimpleGraph}\,V$
  Laman. Then there exists a placement $p : V \to \R^{2}$ at which
  $E(H)$ is row-independent.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{thm:isSparse-exists-rowIndependent-placement}
  Laman implies $(2, 3)$-sparse;
  \cref{thm:isSparse-exists-rowIndependent-placement} applies directly.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The rigidity matroid (dim 2)}
\label{sec:rigidity-matroid-instance}

The relation ``row-LI at some placement'' defines a matroid structure
on the off-diagonal symmetric pairs over $V$. Building it as a mathlib
\texttt{Matroid} requires verifying the four \texttt{IndepMatroid}
axioms; the augmentation axiom is exactly where the combinatorial
$(2, 3)$-count matroid structure does its work, transported through
\cref{thm:edgeSet-rowIndependent-iff-isSparse}.

\begin{definition}[Rigidity matroid in dim $2$]
  \label{def:rigidityMatroid}
  \uses{def:edgeSet-rowIndependent}
  Let $V$ be finite. The \emph{rigidity matroid} of $V$ in dimension
  $2$ is the matroid on the ground set $E(K_{V})$ (the off-diagonal
  symmetric pairs over $V$) whose independent sets are the edge sets
  $I \subseteq E(K_{V})$ that are row-independent at some placement
  $p : V \to \R^{2}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Independent sets of the rigidity matroid are exactly the $(2, 3)$-sparse sets]
  \label{thm:rigidityMatroid-indep-iff-isSparse}
  \uses{def:rigidityMatroid, def:isSparse,
    thm:edgeSet-rowIndependent-iff-isSparse}
  Let $V$ be finite with $|V| \ge 2$ and $I \subseteq E(K_{V})$. Then
  $I$ is independent in the rigidity matroid of $V$ in dimension $2$
  if and only if the spanning subgraph $\mathrm{fromEdgeSet}\,I$ of
  $K_{V}$ is $(2, 3)$-sparse.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  \uses{thm:edgeSet-rowIndependent-iff-isSparse}
  Immediate from \cref{thm:edgeSet-rowIndependent-iff-isSparse} at
  $G = K_{V}$.
\end{proof}
